\section{Introduction}
In automata theory, promise problems, an extension of traditional language recognition where the input is guaranteed to belong to a specific subset of strings, have been predominantly explored in the setting of quantum automata. Over many years, various flavors of promise problems were proposed where quantum automatons give an advantage over the classical counterpart.  Classic examples of promise problems which a quantum computer solves faster than any classical computer are the Deutsch-Jozsa~\cite{deutsch1992rapid}, Simon's~\cite{simon1997power}, and Shor's period-finding problems~\cite{shor1994algorithms}. Here, a quantum computer can demonstrate advantage by achieving exponential reductions in query complexity compared to classical algorithms. Such quantum advantages can serve as tests of quantumness, especially when the problems are designed so that classical algorithms cannot efficiently exploit the promise. Although Shor's algorithm offers a strong theoretical benchmark, practical tests of quantumness often rely on smaller-scale promise problems that are easier to implement and verify on near-term quantum devices.

In this direction, over the past two decades, researchers have explored memory-limited promise problems, where quantum automata outperform the classical one even at small system sizes\cite{ambainis2012superiority} \mari{cite more papers}. A prominent example is the EQ$^*$ problem, where the input is a binary string promised to either have an equal number of $0$'s and $1$'s or differ by a fixed margin. While a quantum finite automaton (QFA) with only a constant number of quantum states (a qutrit) can solve this problem deterministically, any classical deterministic or probabilistic finite automaton (DFA or PFA) would require memory that scales with the input length~\cite{}\mari{cite!}. These results highlight a different kind of quantum advantage, a separation in space complexity, and offer a path toward demonstrating quantumness in low-resource regimes where full quantum algorithms may not yet be feasible. \textit{Can such an advantage be used to test the correct functioning of quantum devices in question?} 

Similar questions have previously been explored in fully device-independent settings, specifically within the framework of multi-prover and verifier interactions, commonly referred to as the Bell game~\cite{bell1964einstein, brunner2014bell}. In this scenario, the quantum advantage, given by the maximal winning probability~\cite{cirel1980quantum}, enables sound certification (i.e., self-testing~\cite{vsupic2020self}), of the maximally entangled state and the anticommuting measurement settings. The self-testing is the strongest notion of certification as it uniquely identifies the inner-workings of the quantum devices in the experiment up to the basis choice.
Recently, some of us proposed \textit{Quantum System Quizzing }(QSQ), the first sound and robust certification protocol for quantum gates in a semi-device-independent setting, under the assumption of bounded quantum memory~\cite{noller2024classical,noller2024self}. In the QSQ protocol, several simple instances of question sets (quizzes) are constructed that a multi-qubit quantum computer can answer correctly, but no classical computer with the same memory capacity can solve. Thus, the QSQ protocol relies on dimension advantage that quantum devices offer.  

In this contribution, we establish that all the quizes considered in the certification protocol are instances of promise problems that separate quantum and classical bounded-memory devices. We shift the attention to the theory of the promise problems and deterministic finite automata to identify sets of sequences which one the one hand give a higher dimension separation, but can also result into self testing protocols. This way we provide a practical framework for certifying quantum advantage under realistic constraints in a more general framework. 
We start analysis from a single-qubit quantum finite automata and extend the results to the multi-qubit and a higher dimensional cases. \section{Deterministic finite state automata and quantum finite state automata}~\label{preliminaries}


Automata theory is a foundational discipline in computer science, seemingly of theoretical nature due to its importance in complexity theory, but actually with many practical implications, not only to model a simple on-off switch or traffic lights, but is an essential part of a compiler of programming languages through the ability to model and verify formal languages. Given the historical development of quantum computing, how its potential and importance were discovered in the dawn of quantum computing through complexity theoretical discoveries, of Simon's or Shor's algorithm, later grouped as the hidden group problems, it seems just natural to also study the field of quantum finite-state automata carefully. We review basic concepts of \ac{DFA}s along the seminal book~\cite{hopcroft2001introduction} Hopcroft et al. And then introduce the quantum generalization, the so-called \ac{QFA}.
Here, we align our notations with reference \cite{zheng2017promise}, which delivers a comprehensive review of \ac{QFA}s, especially regarding state complexity advantages, also called succinctness, of promise problems. 

First, we introduce the formal definition of deterministic finite automata (DFA)
\begin{definition}\label{def:dfa} A deterministic finite automaton (DFA) $\mathcal{A}$ is specified by a 5-tuple 
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{A} = (S, \Sigma, \delta, s_0, S_a) \, ,
\end{equation}
where $S$ is the finite set of states; $\Sigma$ the finite set of input symbols; $s_0 \in S$ the initial state; $S_a \subseteq S$ the set of accepting or final  states, and $\delta$ the transition function $\delta: S \times \Sigma \rightarrow S$ specifying the state transition given an input symbol $\sigma \in  \Sigma$ and the current state. Any path induced by $\delta$ and the input string, also called word, $w \in \Sigma^*$ is deterministic. 
\end{definition}
A graphical description of Def. \ref{def:dfa} is given in Fig. (\todo{draw example}). 
Every DFA $\mathcal{A}$ describes a language $L$, a set of words 
\begin{definition} A DFA $\mathcal{A}$ defines a language $L$
    \begin{equation}
    L(\mathcal{A}) = \{ w | \delta(s_o,w) \in S_a, w \in \Sigma^* \} \, .
\end{equation}
\end{definition}
Inversely, we can also ask if a specific DFA recognizes a language $L$
\begin{definition} (Language recognition)
    A language $L$ over an alphabet $\Sigma$ is \textit{recognized} by a DFA $\mathcal{A}$ if for every $w \in \Sigma^*$
    \begin{equation}
        w \in L \iff \delta (s_0,w) \in S_a
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}
        w \notin L \iff \delta (s_0,w) \notin S_a \quad .
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

It has been shown that the simplest quantum generalizations of a \ac{DFA}, the \ac{MO-1QFA}, does not exhibit advantages regarding the recognition power of languages, in contrary, is only capable of recognizing a subset of the regular languages \cite{brodsky1999characterizations}, which are group languages. One direction of quantum finite state automata research focused on devising further generalization of \ac{QFA} by including on top of the the simples rule of the \ac{MO-1QFA} additional onec that are still within the realistic setting of a quantum computer to close the gat between the recognition power of \ac{DFA} and \ac{QFA}, also asking the question wether \acp{QFA} can surpass the \ac{DFA} like as it is in the sense of other merits. Within this, severel automata has been proposed: Measrure-many quantum finite-state automata, generatlized qfa were transiotions are not strictly unitari but CPTP maps, and also 2-way quantum finite state automata which can recognize beyond than regualr languages \cite{li2009characterizations}. Yet, there is a class of porblems that also the most simple \ac{MO-1QFA} show clear advantages comparaed to the classical \ac{DFA} but advantages in state complexities of \textit{promise problems}, qhich is a a constrained version of the language recognition problem. Let us get acoustumted to this problem class in terms of the classical \ac{DFA} and  first introduce the promise version DFA (pvDFA)
\begin{definition}
    A promise version deterministic finite automaton is specified by a 6-tuple 
    \begin{equation}
        \mathcal{A} = (S, \Sigma, \delta, s_0, S_{yes}, S_{no})
    \end{equation} where $S_{yes}$ and  $S_{no}$ are sets of states with $S_{yes} \cap S_{no} = \emptyset$ and $S_{yes} \cup S_{no} \subseteq S$. Definitions of the remaining symbols are given in Def.~\ref{def:dfa}.
\end{definition}
As we exclusively study promise version DFAs in this work, we refer to pvDFA simply as DFA when not specified.
In past works \cite{zheng2017promise}, promise problems have been studied in two flavours, one being the rocognizability:
\begin{definition} (Promise problem in recognition mode)
        A promise problem $A = (A_{yes}, A_{no})$, with $A_{yes} \cap A_{no} = \emptyset$ and $A_{yes} \cup A_{no} \subseteq \Sigma^*$ is recognized by a DFA $\mathcal{A}$ if for every $w \in \Sigma^* $ it holds
    \begin{equation}
        w \in A_{yes} \iff \delta(s_0, w) \in S_{yes}
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}
        w \in A_{no} \iff \delta(s_0, w) \in S_{no} \quad .
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}
And the second the solvability:
\begin{definition} (Promise problem in solvation mode)
    A promise problem $A = (A_{yes}, A_{no})$, with $A_{yes} \cap A_{no} = \emptyset$ and $A_{yes} \cup A_{no} \subseteq \Sigma^*$ is solved by a DFA $\mathcal{A}$ if for every $w \in A_{yes} \cup A_{no} $ it holds
    \begin{equation}
        w \in A_{yes} \Rightarrow \delta(s_0, w) \in S_{yes}
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}
        w \in A_{no} \Rightarrow \delta(s_0, w) \in S_{no} \quad .
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

Ref.~\cite{zheng2017promise} demonstrated some example to make the difference in these modes more distinct, i.e., a promise porblem based on a non-regular language that can be recognized by a \ac{MO-1QFA} but --- since non-regular --- not with any \ac{DFA}. Regarding the solvability mode, there have been a curious observation: there are many promise porblems, which are solvable with both kinds of automata, but the \ac{MO-1QFA} solves the promise problem with much less states, sometimes even with exponentioally less states.
A popular example of such a promise problem is given by $\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$ \cite{rashid2014implications} with its compelling succinctness result for quantum finite automata \cite{ambainis2012superiority}:
\begin{definition} (Promise problem $\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$)
    The promise problem $\mathtt{EVENODD^k} = \\ (\mathtt{EVENODD^k_{yes}}, \mathtt{EVENODD^k_{no}})$ comprises following sets of unary input strings
    \begin{equation}
        \mathtt{EVENODD^k_{yes}} = \{ \sigma^{i2^k} | i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{even}} \}
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}
        \mathtt{EVENODD^k_{no}} = \{ \sigma^{i2^k} | i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{odd}} \}
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}
Along $\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$, we will get accustomed to the computational power of unary DFAs.
\begin{theorem}\label{theorem:originalpp}
A DFA that solves the promise problem $\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$ requires at least $2^{k+1}$ states.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof} (Adapted from \cite{bianchi2014complexity})
    Since DFAs only allow deterministic transitions, i.e., a unique transition leaving each state, every unary DFA consists of an initial ``tail'' with length $t$ before entering a ``loop'' with length $l$; $t + l = |S|$. $t$, as well as $l$, may be set to zero, although in case of input strings length larger than $|S|$, $l$ at least one to ensure well-defined outputs for such strings. Such a unary DFA implements a modulo $l$ operation with offset $t$ on the length of the input string. Hence, $\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$ is solved if a DFA $\mathcal{A}$ can ensure 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:modcond}
    \{ (i2^k - t)\bmod{l} \,|\, i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{even}} \} \cap
    \{ (i2^k - t)\bmod{l} \,|\, i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{odd}} \} = \emptyset .
\end{equation} 
First we show that a DFA with $l = 2^{k+1}$ and $t=0$ indeed solves $\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$. 

Since,
\begin{align}
    \{ i\cdot2^k\bmod{2^{k+1}} \,|\, i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{even}} \} &= 
    \{j \cdot 2^{k+1} \bmod{2^{k+1}} \,|\, j \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0} \} \\ &= \{0, 0, \dots\}  \\
    \{ i\cdot2^k\bmod{2^{k+1}} \,|\, i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{odd}} \} &= 
    \{(j \cdot 2^{k+1} - 2^{k})\bmod{2^{k+1}} \,|\, j \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0} \} \\ &= \{2^{k}, 2^{k}, \dots\}    
\end{align} 

Eq.~\ref{eq:modcond} is clearly fulfilled and a DFA $\mathcal{A}$ with $S_{yes} = \{s_0\}$ and  $S_{no} = \{s_{2^k}\}$ categorizes the tested input strings correctly.

Next, let us study tail-free DFAs with $|S| < 2^{k+1}$. First, consider the case of $l$ odd. The input sequence with length $l \cdot 2^{k}$ is contained in $\texttt{EVENODD}_{no}^{k}$. Since $l \cdot 2^{k} \bmod{l} = 0$ but $0 \in \texttt{EVENODD}_{yes}^{k}$ always, this fails to solve the promise problem.
For $l$ even, we factorize $l$ into $l = 2^{n} \cdot p$, where $p$ consists of only odd factors. If $p = 1$, $l$ divides $2^k$ such that sequences from both sets will be sent to the accepting state. For $p \neq 1$, we observe that the sequence with length $p\cdot2^k = p \cdot 2^n \cdot 2^{k-n} \in \texttt{EVENODD}_{no}^{k}$ which can be divided by $l$, causing a contradiction, too. Hence, no tail-free DFAs with  $|S| < 2^{k+1}$ solve this promise problem.

In the following, we assume $t \neq 0$. The tail has two effects on the outputs of the DFA; $t$ introduces a constant offset in the outputs of the DFA $i \cdot 2^k - t \bmod l = i \cdot 2^k\bmod l - t \bmod l$ and secondly sends sequences with length $\leq t$ to distinct states which can be labelled freely. By applying similar argumentations as the tail-free case, we can show that there is no DFA with $|S| < 2^{k+1}$ solving the promise problem.

First, consider $l$ odd. Then, $l \cdot 2^{k} \in \texttt{EVENODD}_{no}^{k}$ and  $l \cdot 2^{k} - t \bmod l = - t \bmod l$. Likewise, we know that $2 \cdot l \cdot 2^{k} \in \texttt{EVENODD}_{yes}^{k}$ and $2\cdot l \cdot 2^{k} - t \bmod l = - t \bmod l$.

If $l$ even, we again consider $l = 2^{n} \cdot p$. Then, 
$p \cdot 2^n \cdot 2^{k-n} - t \bmod l = - t \bmod l$. However $2 \cdot p \cdot 2^n \cdot 2^{k-n} \in \texttt{EVENODD}_{yes}^{k}$ with same output $- t \bmod l$.

The smallest DFA solving $\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$ indeed $|S| = 2^{k+1}$ with $l = 2^{k+1}$. A DFA with tail $t \neq 0$ still requires a loop with $l = 2^{k+1}$.
\end{proof}

$\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$ is a costly task in the DFA setting. Next, we introduce the \ac{QFA}, more specifically the \textit{measure-once quantum finite state automaton} (MO-QFA) and reconstruct the result from Ref.~\cite{ambainis2012superiority} that showed that QFAs need only a constant number of states to solve $\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$, which is precisely 2.

\begin{definition}
The measure-once quantum finite state automaton (MO-QFA) $\mathcal{M}$ is specified by a 6-tuple
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{M} = (Q, \Sigma, \{U_\sigma \,|\, \sigma \in \Sigma \}, \ket{0}, Q_{yes}, Q_{no}) ,
\end{equation}
where $Q = \{\, \ket{i} \,|\, 0\leq i\leq|Q|\,\}$ is the finite set of orthonormal quantum states, $\Sigma$ the finite set of input symbols, $U_\sigma$ unitary operator analogous to the classical transition functions, $\ket{0}$ the initial state and finally the yes- and no-labeled states $Q_{yes}$ and $Q_{no}$. An input string $w = \sigma_1 \sigma 2, \dots,\sigma_n$ initiates the application of the unitary gates $U_w = U_{\sigma_n}\cdots U_{\sigma_2} U_{\sigma_1} $ which map the initial state to $\ket{\psi_w} = U_w \ket{0}$. The membership of $\ket{\psi_w}$ is evaluated through the projective operators $P_{yes} = \sum_{\ket{i} \in Q_{yes}} \ket{i} \bra{i}$ and $P_{no} = \sum_{\ket{i} \in Q_{no}} \ket{i} \bra{i}$.
\end{definition}
Note that we immediately introduced the promise version of the MO-QFA.
We can next construct a simple example that solves $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$ succinctly \cite{ambainis2012superiority,geffert2014classical,rashid2014implications}.
\begin{theorem}
 An MO-QFA can solve $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$ with $|Q| = 2$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
    Consider the following MO-QFA
    \begin{align}
        \mathcal{M} = ( Q &= \{\ket{+},\,\ket{-} \},\, \Sigma = \{\sigma\}, \{U_\sigma\},\, \ket{0},\, \\ Q_{yes} &= \{\ket{+}\},\, Q_{no} = \{\ket{-}\} ) 
    \end{align}
    \begin{equation}
        U_\sigma = \sqrt[2^k]{Z} = 
        \begin{pmatrix}
            1 & 0\\ 
            0 & \sqrt[2^k]{-1}
\end{pmatrix}.  
    \end{equation}
    Then, $U_\sigma^{i\cdot2^k} = I$ for $i$ even and $U_\sigma^{i\cdot2^k} = Z$ for $i$ odd. Thus, the initial state $\ket{+}$ will be mapped to the respective outcomes of the promise problem $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$.
\end{proof}

From the succinctness result in the unary unary promise problems $\mathtt{EVENODD_k}$ we can meke the following obervation. The smallest solution DFA to the promise problem is equivalent to the transition graph spanned in the Bloch sphere \todo{(ref fig)} generated by the gate $\sqrt[2^k]{Z}$, and the initial state $$\ket{+}$$. Motivated by this observation, we ask if things work the other way around, we can define a promise problem from specific quantum gates and initial states, using well-established tools and arguments from automata theory?

We first attempt at a clean procedure how to define a \ac{DFA} from specific quantum gates and a initial state.

For the beginning we will focus only on finitie groups that are geneerated from certain qubit gates.

\begin{definition} (Defining a DFA from a finite group QFA)
Let $\mathcal{M}$ be a MO-1QFA that is definided via the initial state $\ket{0}$, which is also the accept state, the reject state wich is given by the orthogonal $\bra{0}\ket{r} = 0$ and unitary gates $U_{\sigma_i}$, $\sigma_i \in \Sigma$ which are generators of a finite group. Then, we can construct a DFA from this QFA by associating the accept and reject states, but also the intermediate states during gate application as states in the DFA sense. More concrete, we define from such a QFA $\mathcal{M}$ a DFA $\mathcal{A} = (S, \Sigma, \delta, s_0, S_{yes}, S_{no})$, where $s_0 \equiv \ket{0}$, $S_{yes} = \{ s_0 \}$, $s_r \equiv \ket{r}$, $S_{no} = \{ s_r \}$, and non-final states $s_i \in S$, where each $s_i$ is associated with a unique state which is generated by any group elements. Since we assuemed finite groups, we will also obtain a finite graph. The transition function $\delta$ will be a list documenting which generator maps the current state to which other state.
\end{definition}

Note, few things: the automaton is defined via a group - that means, for any word $w \in \Sigma$ on any state $s_i$, we have an inverse word $w^{-1}$ such that $\delta(s_i,w w^{-1})=s_1$, where the inverse element itself is unique such that all inverse words are equivalenet to that inverse element. Such languages are called group languages, and it is known that the langueages a \ac{MO-1QFA} can recognize are exactly the group languages \cite{brodsky1999characterizations}. Also we could have stareted directly from the Cayley graph of the generators to define the DFA. We associate each node of the Cayley graph with a state in the DFA and the transistions are also given already. However, in our setting the interplay with the initial states are cruicial, e.g. if we for instance chose as the initial state some non-trivial stabilizer state, this would cause the Cayley graph to have repetitevely the same states. Thish would not ultimately change anything, but we wanted to start directly with the "most compact" DFA.


\begin{definition}
    From Ref.~\cite{cayley1878desiderata} - A Cayley graph $C(G,X)$ is a directed graph defined from a group $G$ and its generator $X$. Vertices of $C$ are the elements $g \in G$ and edges are defined for each $x \in X$ and $g$.
\end{definition}

If we label the initial element with $\ket{+}$, and choose $X = \{\sqrt[2^k]{Z}\}$, the resulting Cayley graph is the cyclic graph with length $2^{k+1}$. Introducing accept and reject states $G_{yes}$ and $G_{no}$ makes this Cayley graph also a DFA $\mathcal{C} = (S \equiv G, \Sigma \equiv X, \delta \equiv G \times X \rightarrow G, s_0 \equiv \ket{+}, S_{yes} \equiv G_{yes}, S_{no} \equiv G_{no})$. The transition function $\delta(s_k,w)$ becomes equivalent to $ w^{inverse}\ket{s_k}$, where the superscript indicates reversed gate order, preserving the left-to-right reading intuition. Henceforth, we omit this superscript but implicitly assume the reversed order when applying a word to a state.

We move next to how to define a promise problem from a given DFA.

\begin{definition}\label{def:dfainducedpp} (DFA-induced promise problem) Let $\mathcal{A}$ be a DFA. Then a DFA-induced promise problem is given as $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A}) = (L_{yes}(\mathcal{A}), L_{no}(\mathcal{A}))$, where the languages, the yes- and the no-lanugage are induced by $\mathcal{A}$
    \begin{equation}
        L_{yes}(\mathcal{A}) = \{ w | w \in \Sigma^* , \delta(s_0,w) \in S_{yes}\},
    \end{equation}
    and the no-language
    \begin{equation}
        L_{no}(\mathcal{A}) = \{ w | w \in \Sigma^* , \delta(s_0,w) \in S_{no}\}.
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

What would be the state complexity of a DFA that solves such a promise problem? 
The trivial solution is given by the promise problem inducing \ac{DFA} - this would be definitely a solution to the promise porblem. However is it the smallest?

THe solution to this question is closely tied to the DFA minimization problem, which has been studied in early days of automata theory. This discepline concernced with equivalence of automata, and minimal instances among equivalent \acp{DFA}.  Let's review these concepts, by summarizing the section "Equivalence and minimization of Automata" of Ref.~\cite{hopcroft2001introduction}.

Beofre discussing equivalences of two DFAs, first discuss the equivalences of states withing the same \ac{DFA}. 

\begin{definition} (Equivalence of two states, from \cite{hopcroft2001introduction})
    Two states of a given \ac{DFA} $\mathcal{A}$, $s_i, s_j \in S$ are considered equivalent, if $s_i$ and $s_j$ both eigther accepting or both rejecting and also for all $w \in \Sigma^*$, $\delta(s_i,w)$ and $\delta(s_j,w)$ both accepting or both rejecting. Then, we say  $s_i, s_j$ are \textit{indistinguishable}. If there is a $w \in \Sigma^*$ that maps $s_i, s_j$ to differently labeled states, we say  $s_i, s_j$ are \textit{distinguishable}. 
\end{definition}

Ref.~\cite{hopcroft2001introduction} also introduced a algroithm to compute all dinstinguishable pairs within a \ac{DFA}, called the \textit{the table-filling algorithm}. 

Given an \ac{DFA} $\mathcal{A} = (S, \Sigma, \delta, s_0, S_{a})$ the following algorithm will identify all indistinguishable and distinguishable states:
\begin{enumerate}[Step 1:]
    \item Generate a table containing all unique pair of states of $\mathcal{A}$
    \item place an $X$ to such pairs $p,q$ such that $p \in S_a$ and $q \in S \setminus S_a$ or vice versa
    \item for all pairs marked via $X$ and all input letters $\sigma \in \Sigma$ find pairs $r,s$ such that $\delta (r,\sigma) = p$ and $\delta (s,\sigma) = q$. Place $x$ on the pair $(r,s)$, if not already there.
    \item repeat Step 3 until no new $x$ are placed
\end{enumerate}

Having introduced the notion of minimal \ac{DFA} carefully, we can establish some results regarding to the DFA-induced promise problems from direct implication from minimality.

\begin{proposition}
    If the promise problem $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A})$ is induced from a minimal automaton, $\mathcal{A}$ is the smallest automaton to recognize this promise problem.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    When we defined the \ac{DFA} induced promise problem in \cref{def:dfainducedpp}, we defined the yes-language to be the language that is accepted via the \ac{DFA} $\mathcal{A}$. By \cref{thr:minimaldfa}, then if $\mathcal{A}$ is minimal, there is no smaller \ac{DFA} to recognize the yes-language. Same holds for the no-language --- here when we define the lanugage, we implicitely relabeled the sole reject state to be the final state and treated all other states as non-finial. We can treat the no-language thus equivalent to the yes-language.
\end{proof}

Consider a cyclic graph of length 6 with the initial state as accepting and the third state as rejecting. This induces the promise problem $\mathtt{PP} = (\{0,6,12,\dots\},\{3,9,15,\dots\})$. Next, we argue the minimality of the language defining DFAs, and we first start with the yes-language. In the yes language mode, the only final state is the initial state, and in the Hopcroft minimality, all other states are treated as rejects. Then, all non-final states are distinguishable from the initial state and from each other because it is always possible to find a word that maps one of the investigated state pairs to the initial state. Hence, such a DFA would be minimal. However, from intuitions we gathered previously, we know that this unary promise problem is solvable with just two states since a $mod 2$ operation alone is sufficient to differentiate between the yes- and no-language. 

The oversimplification was assuming all non-final states must be reject states. While the cycle length 6 automaton is indeed minimal when producing exactly this language, the promise problem in solvation mode poses a different question: since in solvation, we restrict the input that is ever given to the automaton is the promise languages, it is never requirested that the automaton is accepting or reject only the promise languages. \begin{enumerate}[Step 1:]

    \item generate a table containing all  pair of states of $\mathcal{A}$
    \item place an $\mathtt{x}$ between $s_0$ and $s_r$, our seed pair
    \item for all pairs marked via  $\mathtt{x}$  and all input letters $\sigma \in \Sigma$ find pairs $s_i,s_j$ such that $\delta (s_i,\sigma) = s_0$ and $\delta (s_j,\sigma) = s_r$. Place $\mathtt{x}$ on the pair $(s_i,s_j)$, if not already there.
    \item repeat Step 4 until no new $\mathtt{x}$ are placed
    \item if there are unmarked pairs left, pick one pair, call them the new seed pair and repeat steps 4 -5 but change the color of the marks. each color define cluster of pairs.
    \item repeat step 6. until all pairs are marked

    \item $\mathtt{x}$ marked paires give rise to inequivalence relation and other colored pairs to potetntial equivalence relations. For each cluster, compute satisfyerbility regarding the inequality relations.
    \item if satisfyerble group equivalent states to blocks
    \item repeat step what ever also for all combinations clusters that are compatible with the inequality relations
    \item compute also the for all combinations the equality blocks
    \item return the equivalent relation defining cluster or the combination of clusters that leads to the smallest number of blocks
    \item merge states according to these blocks
    \item update transition function accordingly
    \item return the automaton
\end{enumerate}

In recognition mode, all inputs $w\in \Sigma^*$ maintain an if-and-only-if relation to the promise, where words outside the promise reach unlabeled states distinct from both $S_{yes}$ and $S_{no}$. In solvation mode, we assume inputs will never fall outside the promised sets, allowing us to disregard outputs for words beyond our promised inputs. \begin{exampl}
    Consider the promise problem induced by $C = (\{ \ket{+},\ket{+_y},\ket{-},\ket{-_y},\ket{0},\ket{1} \}, \{s,h\})$ (see. Fig.) \todo{add fig}, that is 
    \begin{align}
                \mathtt{PP}_{yes}(\mathcal{C}) &= \{w \, | \, w \in \Sigma^* \land w \ket{+} = \ket{+} \} \\

    \end{align}
    The promise problem inducing Cayley graph is minimal in solvation mode and is thus the smallest DFA solving the promise problem.
\end{exampl}

\begin{proof}
    We use the modified version of Hopcroft's algorithm to argue the minimality of the Cayley graph. States $\ket{+}$ and $\ket{-}$ are final states with definite labels, and we show for all possible label assignments of the remaining states $\ket{+_y},\ket{-_y},\ket{0},\ket{1}$ that the DFA version of the Cayley graph is minimal, i.e. all states are reachable and distinguishable. Reachability is obviously given; we proceed immediately to the check of distinguishability. We iterate over all state pairs and argue their distinguishability. 
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $\ket{+}$ and $\ket{+_y}$: If $\ket{+_y} \in S_{no}$, distinguishable under the empty word; if $\ket{+_y} \in S_{yes}$, $\ket{+}\xrightarrow{s} \ket{+_y}$, and $\ket{+_y}\xrightarrow{s} \ket{-}$. \textit{Distinguishable}.
        
        \item $\ket{+}$ and $\ket{-}$: $\ket{-} \in S_{no}$, distinguishable under the empty word. \textit{Distinguishable}.
        
       \item $\ket{+}$ and $\ket{-_y}$: If $\ket{-_y} \in S_{no}$, distinguishable under the empty word; if $\ket{-_y} \in S_{yes}$, $\ket{-_y}\xrightarrow{s} \ket{+}$, and $\ket{+}\xrightarrow{s} \ket{+_y}$. If $\ket{+_y} \in S_{no}$, distinguishable, else $\ket{-_y}\xrightarrow{ss} \ket{+_y}$ and $\ket{+}\xrightarrow{ss} \ket{-}$. \textit{Distinguishable}.

        \item $\ket{+_y}$ and $\ket{-_y}$:  $\ket{+_y}\xrightarrow{s} \ket{-}$, and $\ket{-_y}\xrightarrow{s} \ket{+}$. \textit{Distinguishable}.

        \item $\ket{-}$ and $\ket{+_y}$ or $\ket{-_y}$: analogous to $\ket{+}$ and $\ket{+_y}$ or $\ket{-_y}$. \textit{Distinguishable}.

        \item $\ket{0}$ and $\ket{+}, \ket{+_y}, \ket{-_y}, \ket{-}$: applying $s^*$ on $\ket{0}$ leaves this state invariant. We can always distinguish these states using an appropriate unary $s$ word. \textit{Distinguishable}.

        \item $\ket{1}$ and $\ket{+}, \ket{+_y}, \ket{-_y}, \ket{-}$: analogous to the case above. \textit{Distinguishable}.

        \item $\ket{0}$ and $\ket{1}$: $\ket{0}\xrightarrow{h} \ket{+}$, and $\ket{1}\xrightarrow{h} \ket{-}$. \textit{Distinguishable}.
        
        \end{itemize}
        
        All states are distinguishable in the solvation mode, and thus, the solution to the induced promise problem.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Non-unary promise problems: $\texttt{DIOF}$}

Ref.~\cite{bianchi2014complexity} discusses a further generalization of $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$ which its non-unary extension $\mathtt{DIOF^{a,N}_{r_1,r_2}}$.

\begin{definition}
    The promise problem $\mathtt{DIOF^{a,N}_{r_1,r_2}}=(\mathtt{DIOF^{a,N}_{r_1,r_2,yes}},\mathtt{DIOF^{a,N}_{r_1,r_2,no}})$ comprises following sets of multi-letter input strings
    \begin{align}
       \mathtt{DIOF^{a,N}_{r_1,r_2,yes}} = \{ w \in \Sigma^* | (a_1|w|_{\sigma_1} + a_2|w|_{\sigma_2} + \dots + a_H|w|_{\sigma_H}) \equiv r_1 \bmod{N}\} \\
        \mathtt{DIOF^{a,N}_{r_1,r_2,no}} = \{ w \in \Sigma^* | (a_1|w|_{\sigma_1} + a_2|w|_{\sigma_2} + \dots + a_H|w|_{\sigma_H}) \equiv r_2 \bmod{N}\},
    \end{align}
    where the alphabet consists of $H$ letters $\Sigma = (\sigma_1, \dots, \sigma_H)$ with  associated weights $a= (a_1, \dots, a_H)$. Promised input sequences are solutions to some diophantine equations.
\end{definition}
In the following, we discuss a specific incarnation of $\mathtt{DIOF^{a,N}_{r_1,r_2}}$ to maintain the intuition of the promise problems in the quantum case, i.e., the realization of solutions with $2^k$-th root of the $Z$-gate. This is the case for $N=2^{k+1}$, $r_1 = 0$, $r_2 = 2^k$, $H = k$, and $a = (1,2^1,\dots, 2^{k-1})$. We refer to this setting as $\mathtt{DIOF^{k}}$.

\begin{theorem}
    A DFA that solves the promise problem $\mathtt{DIOF^{k}}$ requires at least $2^{k+1}$ states.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    We associate $\sigma_1$ with the single letter in the unary problem $\mathtt{EVENODD_k}$. Input sequences from $\mathtt{EVENODD_k}$ are clearly included in $\mathtt{DIOF^{k}}$, so the lower bound on the state complexity is already known. Left to show is that $2^{k+1}$ states are enough to accommodate for non-unary input sequences. $\sigma_1$ induces a cycle with length $2^{k+1}$. The DFA solves the promise problem if the transition function of other letters are given accordingly to the weights $a_i$, $s_n \xrightarrow{\sigma_i} s_{n+a_i}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}
    An MO-QFA can solve $\mathtt{DIOF^{k}}$ with $|Q|=2$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Consider again the MO-QFA 
      \begin{align}
        \mathcal{M} = ( Q &= \{\ket{+},\,\ket{-} \},\, \Sigma = \{\sigma\}, \{U_\sigma\},\, \ket{0},\, \\ Q_{yes} &= \{\ket{+}\},\, Q_{no} = \{\ket{-}\} ) 
    \end{align}
    \begin{equation}
        U_{\sigma_1} = \sqrt[2^k]{Z} = 
        \begin{pmatrix}
            1 & 0\\ 
            0 & \sqrt[2^k]{-1}
\end{pmatrix}.  
    \end{equation} Additionally, we define further unitaries
    \begin{equation}
        U_{\sigma_2} = \sqrt[2^{k-1}]{Z}, \dots, U_{\sigma_{k}} = \sqrt{Z}.
    \end{equation}   
    The weights faithfully represent the conversion of each letter $\sigma_i$ to $\sigma_1$, and since all operators commute this MO-QFA solves $\mathtt{DIOF^{k}}$.
\end{proof}


\section{Quantum-inspired promise problems}

We have reviewed promise problems that have been studied in the past in the context of state complexity advantages between DFAs and MO-QFAs. Starting from such problems, we can formulate further promise problems from known quantumly simple modifications.

We suspect that finding the minimal state automaton that solves the promise problem is closely related to the "separating words problem" \cite{robson1989separating, demaine2011remarks,chase2020new}.

\subsection{Generalized promise problems}

Up to now, we have looked at promise problems, which are, per definition, dichotomic. 
Can we alter the state dimension gap between classical and quantum DFA by moving to multichotomous promise problems? In the quantum quizzing protocol, only sequences with finite length can be executed to remain practicable. How does the limitedness of the input sequences change the succinctness of required DFAs?  We define such a promise problem by expanding the previous $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$.

\subsection{Multichotomous promise problems}
\begin{definition}
    The generalized promise problem $\mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_q = (\mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_0,\mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_1, \dots, \mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_{r-1})$ with $q$ prime comprises the following unary input strings
    \begin{align}
       \mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_0 &= \{ \sigma^{i\cdot r} | i \equiv 0\ (\bmod q)\} \\
       \mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_1 &= \{ \sigma^{i\cdot r} | i \equiv 1\ (\bmod q)\} \\
       &\vdotswithin{=} \notag \\
       \mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_{q-1} &= \{ \sigma^{i\cdot r} | i \equiv q-1\ (\bmod q )\}
    \end{align}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}
    The generalized promise problem $\mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_q$ can be solved with a $4+q$-tupled DFA $\mathcal{A} = (S,\Sigma,\delta,s_0,S_0,\dots,S_{q-1})$ with
    \begin{equation}
        |S| = q^{m_q + 1},
    \end{equation}
    where $m_q$ the multiplicity of the prime factor $q$ in $r$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Analogous to the proof of Theorem \ref{theorem:originalpp}. If $r$ and $q$ are co-prime, a $\bmod \,q $ operation on the input sequence length is enough to differentiate $\mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_0, \dots,\mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_{q-1}$. This is no longer the case if $q$ occurs in the prime factors of $r$. Express $r = r'\cdot q^{m_q}$, we are looking for $l$ such that $\{ (n\cdot q + 0)\cdot r'\cdot q^{m_q} \bmod{l}\}\cap\{ (n\cdot q + 1)\cdot r'\cdot q^{m_q} \bmod{l}\}\cap\{ (n\cdot q + 2)\cdot r'\cdot q^{m_q} \bmod{l}\} \cap \dots \cap \{ (n\cdot q + q -1)\cdot r'\cdot q^{m_q}\bmod{l} \} = \emptyset$ for all $n\in \mathbb{N}$. 
    
    This is given by $ l = q^{m_q + 1}$: 
    \begin{align*}
        \{ (n\cdot q + 0)\cdot r'\cdot q^{m_q} \bmod q^{m_q+1} \equiv 0 \}&\cap
        \{(n\cdot q + 1)\cdot r'\cdot q^{m_q} \bmod q^{m_q + 1} \equiv m \} \\ \cap 
        \{(n\cdot q + 2)\cdot r'\cdot q^{m_q} \bmod{q^{m_q + 1}} \equiv 2\cdot m \} 
        \cap\dots &\cap \{(n\cdot q + q-1)\cdot r'\cdot q^{m_q} \bmod{q^{m_q + 1}} \equiv (q-1)\cdot m\}
    \end{align*}
    Left to show is that $0 \not\equiv m \not\equiv \dots \not\equiv (q-1)\cdot m$. Note that $ a \equiv b \bmod c \iff k\cdot a \equiv k\cdot b \bmod k\cdot c$. Hence, we can reduce $m \equiv r'\cdot q^{m_q} (\bmod q^{m_q+1})$ to $m' \equiv r' (\bmod q)$. For multiples of $r'$, it holds
    \begin{align*}
        u \cdot r' \equiv v \cdot r'  \Rightarrow (u - v)\cdot r' \equiv 0 ,
    \end{align*}
    and $r'$ and $q$ co-prime by definition. For $u,v < q$, the only solution is $u =w$. Which shows that $\bmod{ q^{m_q + 1}}$ solves the promise problem. Any smaller choice of the modulus $l$ causes a contradiction because $l\cdot r \equiv 0$ but $\sigma^{l\cdot r} \not\in \texttt{GEVENODD}^r_0$ unless $q | l$ which would, however, map all sequences to $0$.
    
\end{proof}


\begin{theorem}
    The promise problem $\mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_q$ can be solved by a $4+q$-tupled MO-QFA $\mathcal{M} = (Q, \Sigma, \{U_\sigma \,|\, \sigma \in \Sigma \}, \ket{0}, Q_{0}, \dots, Q_{q-1}) $ with $|Q|=q$. 
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
    Consider the following MO-QFA
        \begin{align}
        \mathcal{M} = ( Q &= \{\ket{X_0},\dots,\ket{X_{q-1}} \},\, \Sigma = \{\sigma\}, \{U_\sigma\},\, \ket{X_0},\, \\ Q_{0} &= \{\ket{X_0}\},\dots, Q_{q-1} = \{\ket{X_{q-1}}\} ) ,
    \end{align}
    where $\ket{X_0},\dots,\ket{X_{q-1}}$ are eigenstates of the $q$-dimensional $X$- or shift operator \cite{pudda2024generalised}
    \begin{equation}
        X_q = 
        \begin{pmatrix}
            0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 1 \\
            1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\
            0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\
            \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\
            0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 & 0 \\
        \end{pmatrix}.
    \end{equation}  
    We choose $U_\sigma := \sqrt[r]{Z_q}$, where
    \begin{equation}
        Z_q = 
        \begin{pmatrix}
            1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\
            0 & \exp{1 \cdot i\frac{2\pi}{q}} & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\
            0 & 0 & \exp{2\cdot i\frac{2\pi}{q}} & \cdots & 0 \\
            \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
            0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & \exp{(q-1)\cdot i\frac{2\pi}{q}}

    \end{equation}
    Then, $\ket{X_0} \xrightarrow{\sqrt[r]{Z_q}^r} \ket{X_1} \xrightarrow{\sqrt[r]{Z_q}^r} \cdots \xrightarrow{\sqrt[r]{Z_q}^r} \ket{X_{q-1}}$ resulting in a MO-QFA uniquely mapping input sequences to corresponding output states.
\end{proof}

We start with the quantum solution to this promise problem to provide intuition.

\begin{theorem}
    The promise problem $\operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD_k}}$ can be solved with a $N-$qubit MO-QFA $\mathcal{M} = (Q, \Sigma, \{U_{\sigma_i|\sigma_i \in \Sigma}\}, \ket{0}, Q_{0,0,\dots,0},Q_{0,0,\dots,1},\dots,Q_{1,1,\dots,1})$ with $|Q| = 2^N$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
    Consider the MO-QFA where classifying states $Q_{b_0,\dots,b_{N-1}}$ are given by the $N-$qubit $X-$bases basis states
    $Q_{0,0,\dots,0} = \ket{++\cdots+}, Q_{0,0,\dots,1} = \ket{++\cdots-},\dots,Q_{1,1,\dots,1} = \ket{--\cdots-}$.
    If we choose $U_{\sigma_i} = \sqrt[2^k]{Z_i}$ we obtain the desired behavior.  
\end{proof}

To accomplish the same task with a classical DFA, we also require a multiplicative scaling compared to the ``single-qubit  " $\mathtt{EVENODD_k}$ dimension requirement.

\begin{theorem}
    A DFA that solves the promise problem $\operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD_k}}$ requires at least $2^{N(k+1)}$ states.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    First, we devise a DFA with $2^{N(k+1)}$ that solves the promise problem. We notice that $\operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD_k}}$ can be solved by counting the number of each letter $\sigma_i$ in modulo $2^{k+1}$. The modulo counting is implemented by a cyclic transition of length $2^{k+1}$. Then, we can label each state with the current gate counts - to represent all possible gate counts of $N$ different gates in $\operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD_k}}$, we can use $N$ base $2^{k+1}$ numbers. If we include all possible $2^{N(k+1)}$ count instances, we can faithfully process all possible input sequences, even beyond the promised inputs.
    Next, we prove there is no smaller DFA that solves the promise problem. If $|S|<2^{N(k+1)}$, there is at least one state $s$ that has at least two count labels, $l_1,l_2 \in $. Let's call the input sequence leading to $s$ with count $l_1$ and $l_2$, $p_1$ and $p_2$, respectively. We call the inverse of an input sequences $p \notin \operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD^k_{0,0,\dots,0}}}$ to be sequences such that  $p^{-1}\cdot p \in  \operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD^k_{0,0,\dots,0}}}$. Then, $p_1 \cdot p_2^{-1}$ is an input that reaches a state that is from the category $S_{0,0,\dots,0}$. However, generally $p_1 \cdot p_2^{-1} \notin \operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD^k_{0,0,\dots,0}}}$ causing a contradiction. The only case where this would be true is if $p_1 = p_2$, which we have excluded. The last step is to repeat the sequence $p_1 \cdot p_2^{-1}$ such that this generic sequence becomes compatible with the promised inputs. This can be achieved in the worst case with $2^k$ repetitions of $p_1 \cdot p_2^{-1}$. Note that it may happen that $2^k\cdot(p_1 \cdot p_2^{-1}) \in \operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD^k_{0,0,\dots,0}}}$. Then, repeat only $2^{k-1}$ times.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Finite $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$}

Promise problems were discussed as an abstract topic in theoretical computer science. However, a practical application has recently been found in the context of device-independent gate certification protocols \todo[inline]{cite Jan's stuff}. 
A crucial modification over the original setting of promise problems is that the set of possible input sequences is kept finite. In the next section, we discuss the effects of finiteness on the state complexity.


\begin{definition}
    The finite-length version of the promise problem is given as $\mathtt{EVENODD_k^{i_{max}}} = (\mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,yes}}, \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,no}})$ 
    \begin{align}
        \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,yes}} &= \{ \sigma^{i2^k} | i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{even}} \land i \leq i_{max}\}
        \\
        \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,no}} &= \{ \sigma^{i2^k} | i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{odd} } \land i \leq i_{max} \}.
    \end{align}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}\label{theorem:finitepp}
    A DFA solving $\mathtt{EVENODD_k^{i_{max}}}$ requires the state dimension 
    \begin{equation}
        |S| = 
        \begin{cases}
            2^{k+1},\, \text{if} \enspace i_{max} \geq 2^{k+1} - 1 \\
            i_{max},\, \text{else}. 
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Since $\mathtt{EVENODD_k^{i_{max}}} \subset \mathtt{EVENODD_k}$, a DFA with $|S| = 2^{k + 1}$ solves the promise problem as per Therorem~\ref{theorem:originalpp}. Therefore, consider the case $i_{max} < 2^{k+1} - 1$. As before, examine the two input sets regarding their length per input sequences in ascending order
    \begin{align}
    \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,yes}} &= \{0, 2\cdot2^k, \dots \} \\
        \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,no}} &= \{1\cdot2^k, 3\cdot2^k, \dots \}.
    \end{align}
    The promise problem is solved if the DFA $\mathcal{A}$ maps the two distinct sets into distinct sets $\mathcal{A}(\mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,yes}}) \cap \mathcal{A}(\mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,no}}) = \emptyset $.
    Let's assume first that $\mathcal{A}$ is a tail-free DFA. Then,
    \begin{align}
        \mathcal{A}(\mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,yes}}) &= \{0, 2\cdot m, \dots \} \\
        \mathcal{A}(\mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,no}}) &= \{1\cdot2^k \bmod l  =: m, 3\cdot m, \dots \}.
\end{align} 

These outputs induce another cycle on the DFA states $0 \rightarrow 1\cdot m \rightarrow 2\cdot m \rightarrow 3 \cdot m \rightarrow \dots$, where $\mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,yes}}$-states and $\mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,no}}$-states are visited alternately. If $l = 2\cdot 2^k$, the output cycle has length $l_o = 2$, consisting of states $0$ and $2^k$ solving the infinite promise problem. If $l<2\cdot 2^k$ and, for simplicity, $l \nmid 2^k$, we know that the first $l_o$ sequences can be correctly represented. Odd $l_o$ would cause a conflict at the $l_o+1$-th sequence.  Since this output cycle is defined on $l$ states with step size $m$, the cycle length is the smallest combination of $p,l_o$ such that $p\cdot l = l_o\cdot m$. This means for $\gcd(l,m)=g$ we have $p \cdot g \cdot l' = l_o \cdot g \cdot m'$, $l_o = l'$. Note that $l_o$ is always odd - the only scenario of $l_o$ even would occur if $l$ is some even number $l = 2^i\cdot l'$ and $\gcd(l,m)$ odd or lower multiplicity than $i$ of the prime factor $2$. However since $2^k = n \cdot l + m \Rightarrow 2^i(2^{k-i} + l') = m$ which means $2^i$ is a common divisor of both $m$ and $l$ and thus $l_o$ odd. Hence, such DFA can only accommodate a promise problem with up to $l_o$ sequences. 
Next, we will see that in case $l_o \neq l$, we can increase the maximum sequence number to $|S|$ by adding a tail of length $t = 1$, such as $l = |S| - 1 $. From $2^k = n \cdot g \cdot l' + g \cdot m' = n\cdot g (l' + m')$ we see that $gcd(l,m)$ can only be power of $2$. Hence, if  $gcd(l,m) \neq 1 \Rightarrow gcd(l-1, \tilde{m}) = 1$. Since the first sequence, the empty sequence can be correctly recognized by the tail state; in total, such DFA can account for $|S|$ sequences. Thus, a promise problem with $i_{max} < 2^{k+1} - 1$ can be solved with a DFA with $|S| = i_{max}$ states.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
If $i_{max} \leq 2^{k+1}-1$, it is enough to consider sequences for $i_{max}$ even
\begin{align}
          \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,yes}} &= \{ \sigma^{i2^k} | i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{even}} \land i \leq i_{max}\} \\
           \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,no}} &= \{ \sigma^{1 \cdot 2^k} \}
\end{align}

and for  $i_{max}$ odd
\begin{align}
          \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,yes}} &= \{ \sigma^{0}, \sigma^{2 \cdot 2^k}\} \\
         \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,no}} &= \{ \sigma^{i2^k} | i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{odd} } \land i \leq i_{max} \}
\end{align}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
    In the proof of Theorem \ref{theorem:finitepp}, we established that $\mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k}}$ for appropriately small $i_{max}$ can be solved with a DFA with $|S| = i_{max}$ states because only the $i_{max}$-th sequence would cause a contradiction in the DFA outputs since it will map onto the same state as the first sequence from the opposite input set.  This also means that omitting sequences from the opposite input set larger than the shortest would not change the required DFA dimension. 
\end{proof}
