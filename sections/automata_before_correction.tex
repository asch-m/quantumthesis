\chapter{Certifying Quantum Computers with Automata Advantage}\label{chap:automaton}

\section{Preface}
This marks the last chapter of this thesis. While the previous chapters were rather application-focused, this final chapter is more theoretical in nature. The quantum computing and quantum information science community unanimously agrees that certification and benchmarking are important \cite{eisert2020quantum}. Whether from an end-user perspective to verify promised specifications from the vendor or from hardware developers validating prototypical setups, methods are needed to quantify quantum device performance. The Gachechiladze group recently developed a complete and sound \ac{SPAM} error-free certification protocol for quantum computers, including initial states, gates, and measurements, named \ac{QSQ} \cite{noller2024self, noller2024classical}. The work on \ac{QSQ} was motivated by the recognition that no existing certification method appears \textit{to have it all}: Randomized benchmarking \cite{knill2008randomized} is complete, accepting all correctly implemented devices without false negatives, but lacks soundness and cannot guarantee the absence of false positives. Quantum process tomography \cite{o2004quantum} is complete and sound, yet suffers from biases caused by \ac{SPAM} errors.

A promising direction emerges from device-independent self-testing \cite{vsupic2020self}. Here, the Bell game \cite{bell1964einstein, brunner2014bell} is used to certify soundly in a black-box scenario the presence of a maximally entangled pair and two anticommuting measurement settings through maximal winning probability. \ac{QSQ} explores in similar spirit a semi-device-independent setting for self-testing \ac{SPAM} error-free sound certification under dimensional assumptions. From deterministic binary outputs alone, users can infer estimates on fidelities via instructed gate sequence executions. These instructions implement computational tasks requiring a higher memory or dimension complexity to execute classically compared to quantum resources.

The following work focuses precisely on this question: What is the nature of these computational tasks with a dimensional advantage? We establish that \ac{QSQ} relies on a notion of quantum advantage that has been investigated in the realm of promise problems for quantum finite-state automata. This chapter guides the reader through automata theory basics and its quantum generalization, promise problems in language recognition, and construction of promise problems for \ac{QSQ} covering single-qubit, multi-qubit, and qudit systems. We develop a simple no-go criterion for quantum models that cannot be certified within the \ac{QSQ} framework.

\section{Deterministic finite state automata and quantum finite state automata}~\label{preliminaries}


Automata theory is a foundational discipline in computer science. Its ability to model and verify formal languages has not only enabled numerous practical applications, from simple systems like switches and traffic lights to programming language compilers, but, most importantly, has shaped the field of complexity theory. Given the historical rise of quantum computing due to breakthroughs in complexity theory, revealing that certain instances of the hidden subgroup problem can be solved exponentially faster than with classical resources, it is natural to seek a deeper understanding of quantum versions of finite-state automata. We review the basic concepts of a \ac{DFA} following the seminal book by Hopcroft et al.~\cite{hopcroft2001introduction}, and introduce its quantum counterpart, the \ac{QFA}. Our notation aligns with Ref.~\cite{zheng2017promise}, which offers a comprehensive review of \ac{QFA}s, particularly their state complexity advantages in promise problems, also known as succinctness. We then explore how this quantum advantage in dimensionality can be applied to quantum model certification. Building on this, we discuss how fundamental properties, such as \ac{DFA} minimality, lead to no-go results for the certification of certain quantum gates.

First, we introduce the formal definition of the deterministic finite automaton (DFA).
\begin{definition}\label{def:dfa} A deterministic finite automaton (DFA) $\mathcal{A}$ is specified by a 5-tuple 
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{A} = (S, \Sigma, \delta, s_0, S_a) \, ,
\end{equation}
where $S$ is the finite set of states; $\Sigma$ the finite set of input symbols; $s_0 \in S$ the initial state; $S_a \subseteq S$ the set of accepting or final  states, and $\delta$ the transition function $\delta: S \times \Sigma \rightarrow S$ specifying the state transition given an input symbol $\sigma \in  \Sigma$ and the current state. Any path induced by $\delta$ and the input string, also called word, $w \in \Sigma^*$ is deterministic. The existence of $\mathtt{accept}$ states implies the existence of $\mathtt{reject}$  states $s \in S \setminus S_a$.
\end{definition}
Every DFA $\mathcal{A}$ describes a language $L$, a set of words:
\begin{definition} A DFA $\mathcal{A}$ defines a language $L$
    \begin{equation}
    L(\mathcal{A}) = \{ w | \delta(s_o,w) \in S_a, w \in \Sigma^* \} \, .
\end{equation}
\end{definition}
Inversely, we can also ask if a specific DFA recognizes a language $L$.
\begin{definition} (Language recognition)
    A language $L$ over an alphabet $\Sigma$ is \textit{recognized} by a DFA $\mathcal{A}$ if for every $w \in \Sigma^*$
    \begin{align}
        w \in L &\iff \delta (s_0,w) \in S_a \, , \\
        w \notin L &\iff \delta (s_0,w) \notin S_a  .
    \end{align}
\end{definition}

It has been shown that the most straightforward quantum generalization of a \ac{DFA}, the \ac{MO-QFA}, which we will introduce later, does not exhibit advantages regarding the recognition power of languages; on the contrary, it is only capable of recognizing a subset of the regular languages, which are so-called group languages \cite{brodsky1999characterizations}. One direction of quantum finite state automata research focused on devising further generalizations of \acp{QFA} by including, on top of the simple rules of the \ac{MO-QFA}, additional ones that are still within the realistic setting of a quantum computer but close the gap between the recognition power of \acp{DFA} and \acp{QFA}, even addressing the question whether \acp{QFA} can surpass the \ac{DFA} in terms of recognition power or in other merits. Within this endeavor \cite{ambainis2006algebraic,brodsky1999characterizations, li2009characterizations,}, several automata have been proposed: \ac{MM-QFA}, that allows multiple masurements, \ac{MO-gQFA}, where transitions are not strictly unitary but \ac{CPTP} maps, and also \ac{2-QFA} with utilizes a tapehead allowed to move in two directions which can recognize languages beyond the regular ones \cite{li2009characterizations}. Yet, there is a class of problems called \textit{promise problems} for which even the simplest \acp{QFA}, the \acp{MO-QFA}, demonstrates clear advantages in state complexity over classical \acp{DFA}. The promise problems we consider here are a constrained version of the language recognition problem. To become familiar with this problem class, we first introduce the \ac{pvDFA} and examine a concrete instance closely. 
\begin{definition}
    A promise version deterministic finite automaton (pvDFA) is specified by a 6-tuple 
    \begin{equation}
        \mathcal{A} = (S, \Sigma, \delta, s_0, S_{yes}, S_{no}),
    \end{equation} where $S_{yes}$ and  $S_{no}$ are sets of states with $S_{yes} \cap S_{no} = \emptyset$ and $S_{yes} \cup S_{no} \subseteq S$. Definitions of the remaining symbols are given in Def.~\ref{def:dfa}. We call $s \in S \setminus (S_{yes}\cup S_{no})$ indefinite states.
\end{definition}
As we exclusively study promise version DFAs in this work, we refer to \ac{pvDFA} simply as \ac{DFA}, unless specified otherwise.
\acp{DFA} can be represented as state diagrams, see for example Fig.~\ref{fig:sautomata}(b), which consist of nodes, some labeled as $\mathtt{accept}$ or $\mathtt{reject}$  states, and arrows indicating deterministic transitions between states. A \ac{DFA} has a start state (denoted graphically by an arrow from nowhere) where computation begins, and a set of $\mathtt{accept}$/$\mathtt{reject}$  states, denoted graphically by double circles and filled circles, respectively. States that are neither $\mathtt{accept}$ nor $\mathtt{reject}$  remain unfilled circles.

In past works \cite{zheng2017promise}, promise problems have been studied in two flavours, one being the recognizability problem:
\begin{definition} (Promise problem in recognition mode)
        A promise problem $A = (A_{yes}, A_{no})$, with $A_{yes} \cap A_{no} = \emptyset$ and $A_{yes} \cup A_{no} \subseteq \Sigma^*$ is recognized by a DFA $\mathcal{A}$ if for every $w \in \Sigma^* $ it holds

        \begin{align}
            w \in A_{yes} &\iff \delta(s_0, w) \in S_{yes} \, , \\
            w \in A_{no} &\iff \delta(s_0, w) \in S_{no} .
        \end{align}
\end{definition}
Secondly, the solvability:
\begin{definition} (Promise problem in solvation mode)
    A promise problem $A = (A_{yes}, A_{no})$, with $A_{yes} \cap A_{no} = \emptyset$ and $A_{yes} \cup A_{no} \subseteq \Sigma^*$ is solved by a DFA $\mathcal{A}$ if for every $w \in A_{yes} \cup A_{no} $ it holds
        \begin{align}
            w \in A_{yes} &\Rightarrow \delta(s_0, w) \in S_{yes} \, , \\
            w \in A_{no} &\Rightarrow \delta(s_0, w) \in S_{no} .
        \end{align}
\end{definition}

Refs.~\cite {ambainis2012superiority,zheng2017promise} make curious observations regarding promise problems in solvation mode. Numerous promise problems can be solved by both types of automata, \ac{DFA} and \ac{MO-QFA}; however, the \ac{MO-QFA} often solves these promise problems using significantly fewer states, in some cases even exponentially fewer.
A famous example of such a promise problem is given by $\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$ \cite{ambainis2012superiority, rashid2014implications}:
\begin{definition} \label{def:evenodd} (Promise problem $\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$)
    The promise problem $\mathtt{EVENODD^k} = \\ (\mathtt{EVENODD^k_{yes}}, \mathtt{EVENODD^k_{no}})$ comprises the following sets of unary input strings
    \begin{equation}
        \mathtt{EVENODD^k_{yes}} = \{ \sigma^{i2^k} | i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{even}} \} \, ,
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}
        \mathtt{EVENODD^k_{no}} = \{ \sigma^{i2^k} | i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{odd}} \} .
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}
Along with $\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$, we will get accustomed to the computational power of unary DFAs.

\begin{theorem}\label{theorem:originalpp}
 (Adapted from \cite{bianchi2014complexity}) A DFA that solves the promise problem $\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$ requires at least $2^{k+1}$ states.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof} 
    Since \acp{DFA} only allow deterministic transitions, i.e., a unique transition leaving each state, every unary DFA consists of an initial ``tail'' with length $t$ before entering a ``loop'' with length $l$; $t + l = |S|$. $t$, as well as $l$, may be set to zero, although in the case of input strings of length larger than $|S|$, $l$ must be at least one to ensure well-defined outputs for such strings. We call a unary DFA with $t=0$ a ``tail-free'' DFA. A unary \ac{DFA} implements a modulo $l$ operation with offset $t$ on the length of the input string.  Hence, $\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$ is solved if a DFA $\mathcal{A}$ can ensure 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:modcond}
    \{ (i2^k - t)\bmod{l} \,|\, i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{even}} \} \cap
    \{ (i2^k - t)\bmod{l} \,|\, i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{odd}} \} = \emptyset .
\end{equation} 
First we show that a DFA with $l = 2^{k+1}$ and $t=0$ indeed solves $\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$. 

Since
\begin{align}
    \{ i\cdot2^k\bmod{2^{k+1}} \,|\, i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{even}} \} &= 
    \{j \cdot 2^{k+1} \bmod{2^{k+1}} \,|\, j \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0} \} \\ &= \{0, 0, \dots\},  \\
    \{ i\cdot2^k\bmod{2^{k+1}} \,|\, i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{odd}} \} &= 
    \{(j \cdot 2^{k+1} - 2^{k})\bmod{2^{k+1}} \,|\, j \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0} \} \\ &= \{2^{k}, 2^{k}, \dots\}    \, ,
\end{align} 

Eq.~\ref{eq:modcond} is clearly fulfilled and a DFA $\mathcal{A}$ with $S_{yes} = \{s_0\}$ and  $S_{no} = \{s_{2^k}\}$ categorizes the tested input strings correctly.

Next, let us study tail-free DFAs with $|S| < 2^{k+1}$. First, consider the case of $l$ odd. The input sequence with length $l \cdot 2^{k}$ is contained in $\mathtt{EVENODD}_{no}^{k}$. Since $l \cdot 2^{k} \bmod{l} = 0$ but $0 \in \mathtt{EVENODD}_{yes}^{k}$ always, this fails to solve the promise problem.
For $l$ even, we factorize $l$ into $l = 2^{n} \cdot p$, where $p$ consists of only odd factors. If $p = 1$, $l$ divides $2^k$ such that sequences from both sets will be sent to the accepting state. For $p \neq 1$, we observe that the sequence with length $p\cdot2^k = p \cdot 2^n \cdot 2^{k-n} \in \mathtt{EVENODD}_{no}^{k}$ which can be divided by $l$, causing a contradiction, too. Hence, no tail-free DFAs with  $|S| < 2^{k+1}$ solve this promise problem.

In the following, we assume $t \neq 0$. The tail has two effects on the outputs of the DFA; $t$ introduces a constant offset in the outputs of the DFA $(i 2^k - t) \bmod l = (i \cdot 2^k\bmod l) - (t \bmod l)$ and secondly sends sequences with length $\leq t$ to distinct states which can be labelled freely. By applying similar argumentations as the tail-free case, we can show that there is no DFA with $|S| < 2^{k+1}$ solving the promise problem.

First, consider $l$ odd. Then, $l \cdot 2^{k} \in \mathtt{EVENODD}_{no}^{k}$ and  $(l 2^{k} - t) \bmod l = - t \bmod l$. Likewise, we know that $2 \cdot l \cdot 2^{k} \in \mathtt{EVENODD}_{yes}^{k}$ and $2\cdot l \cdot 2^{k} - t \bmod l = - t \bmod l$.

If $l$ even, we again consider $l = 2^{n} \cdot p$. Then, 
$p \cdot 2^n \cdot 2^{k-n} - t \bmod l = - t \bmod l$. However $2 p 2^n 2^{k-n} \in \mathtt{EVENODD}_{yes}^{k}$ with same output $- t \bmod l$.

The smallest DFA solving $\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$ indeed $|S| = 2^{k+1}$ with $l = 2^{k+1}$. A DFA with tail $t \neq 0$ still requires a loop with $l = 2^{k+1}$.
\end{proof}

$\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$ is a costly task in the DFA setting, requiring an exponentially scaling state complexity. Next, we finally introduce the \ac{MO-QFA} and reconstruct the result from Ref.~\cite{ambainis2012superiority} that showed that \acp{MO-QFA} need only a constant number of states to solve $\mathtt{EVENODD^k}$, which is precisely two.

\begin{definition}
The measure-once quantum finite state automaton (MO-QFA) $\mathcal{M}$ is specified by a 6-tuple
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{M} = (Q, \Sigma, \{U_\sigma \,|\, \sigma \in \Sigma \}, \ket{0}, Q_{yes}, Q_{no}) ,
\end{equation}
where $Q = \{\, \ket{i} \,|\, 0\leq i<|Q|\,\}$ is the finite set of orthonormal quantum states, $\Sigma$ the finite set of input symbols, $U_\sigma$ unitary operator analogous to the classical transition functions, $\ket{0}$ the initial state and finally the yes- and no-labeled states $Q_{yes}$ and $Q_{no}$. An input string $w = \sigma_1 \sigma_2, \dots,\sigma_n$ initiates the application of the unitary gates $U_w = U_{\sigma_n}\cdots U_{\sigma_2} U_{\sigma_1} $ which map the initial state to $\ket{\psi_w} = U_w \ket{0}$. The membership of $\ket{\psi_w}$ is evaluated through the projective operators $P_{yes} = \sum_{\ket{i} \in Q_{yes}} \ket{i} \bra{i}$ and $P_{no} = \sum_{\ket{i} \in Q_{no}} \ket{i} \bra{i}$ with $P_{yes}P_{no} = 0$ and $P_{yes} + P_{no} = \mathbbm{1}$.
\end{definition}
Note that we immediately introduced the promise version of the MO-QFA. We sometimes drop the MO prefix for conciseness, but imply the measure-once QFA throughout this manuscript.
Following this, we construct the \ac{MO-QFA} that solves $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$ succinctly \cite{ambainis2012superiority,geffert2014classical,rashid2014implications}.

Applying the $s$-gates as instructed in the test sequences to the initial state $\ket{+}$, then performing an $X$-basis measurement, produces the same output as requested by the promise problem.  \textbf{(b)}: The DFA solution to $\mathtt{EVENODD_1}$. \begin{theorem}
 A QFA can solve $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$ with $|Q| = 2$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
    Consider the following QFA
    \begin{align}
        \mathcal{M} = ( Q &= \{\ket{+},\,\ket{-} \},\, \Sigma = \{\sigma\}, \{U_\sigma\},\, \ket{0},\, \\ Q_{yes} &= \{\ket{+}\},\, Q_{no} = \{\ket{-}\} ) 
    \end{align}
    \begin{equation}
        U_\sigma = \sqrt[2^k]{Z} = 
        \begin{pmatrix}
            1 & 0\\ 
            0 & \sqrt[2^k]{-1}
\end{pmatrix}.  
    \end{equation}
    Then, $U_\sigma^{i\cdot2^k} = I$ for $i$ even and $U_\sigma^{i\cdot2^k} = Z$ for $i$ odd. Thus, the initial state $\ket{+}$ will be mapped to the respective outcomes of the promise problem $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$.
\end{proof}

The \ac{QFA} can solve the promise problem $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$ with exponentially fewer states in the classical case. Can we devise an application that goes beyond solving the promise problem and leverages this spatial advantage?

\subsection{Certification from automata advantages: The Quantum System Quizzing Protocol}~\label{subsect:QSQ}


Refs.~\cite{noller2024classical,noller2024self} consider quantum model certification in the scenario where a user communicates classical instructions, a promise problem, to a quantum computer and receives classical responses. The user is able to establish the quality of the implemented computation solely from classical and deterministic input-output relations. To clarify this approach, we describe the protocol and the assumptions imposed on device implementation.


\begin{figure}
   \centering
\includegraphics{images/automata/scenario.pdf}
   \caption{The considered scenario: a quantum system is initialized in a state $\rho$, undergoes a series of transformations specified by classical instructions, a string $\sigma_{i_1},\sigma_{i_2},\dots,\sigma_{i_l}$ consisting of letters $\{\sigma_i\}_{i=0}^{m}$  chosen from a finite set $\mathcal{X}$, and is measured producing an outcome $a\in \mathrm{A}$.}
   \label{fig:scenario}
\end{figure}

A quantum system is prepared in an initial state by a quantum computer, undergoes a series of transformations instructed by an input string or word $w= \sigma_{i_1} \dots \sigma_{i_l} \in \Sigma^*$, where $\Sigma^*$ denotes the set of all strings over the alphabet $\Sigma = \{\sigma_1, \dots \sigma_m\}$, and is finally measured producing the outcome $a\in \mathrm{A}$, with $\mathrm{A}$ being the set of possible outcomes.
Quantum-mechanically, an experiment in this scenario is described by a quantum model, which we define as a tuple $\mathcal{M}=(\rho,\{\Lambda_{\sigma_i}\}_{ \sigma_i \in \Sigma},\{M_a\}_{a\in \mathrm{A}})$, an initial state $\rho$, a set $\{\Lambda_{\sigma_i} \}_{\sigma_i \in \Sigma}$ of quantum channels, and a \ac{POVM} $\{M_{a}\}_{a\in \mathrm{A}}$, all defined over a finite-dimensional Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$. 


Importantly, a user does not have any specification of the quantum model, so the state, evolution, and the measurement are treated as a black box except for the available \textit{degrees of freedom}, otherwise known as the \textit{dimension assumption}.
Another crucial property that the user assumes on the implementation function is that each instruction label $\sigma_i \in \Sigma$ is associated with the corresponding quantum channel $\Lambda_{\sigma_i}$, independently of other operations performed in that experiment, an assumption which we refer to as \emph{context independence}. 
The dimension and context-independence assumptions are not restrictive in comparison to existing tools in the literature, as all other well-established certification or characterization techniques, such as process tomography, \ac{RB} and its many variants, and \ac{GST}, rely on them. On the contrary, these assumptions are minimal in two important ways: first, certification of a single device is impossible without them; and second, all other methods impose additional assumptions beyond these.

Since we are working with classical data obtained from a quantum system, we must discuss the emerging gauge freedom in quantum models. Clearly, the global basis change, also known as the choice of the reference frame, cannot alter statistics. So, we can only hope to identify a quantum model up to unitary (antiunitary) transformations: 

\begin{definition}~\label{def:modelequivalence}
Two fixed-dimensional quantum models $\mathcal{M} = (\rho,\{\Lambda_\sigma\}_{\sigma \in \Sigma},\{M_a\}_{a\in \mathrm{A}})$ and $\mathcal{N}=(\tilde\rho,\{\tilde\Lambda_\sigma \}_{\sigma \in \Sigma},\{\tilde M_a\}_{a\in \mathrm{A}})$ are \emph{equivalent} if -- up to a complex conjugation applied to all components -- there exists a unitary $U$ such that $U\tilde\rho U^\dagger = \rho$, $U\tilde M_a U^\dagger = M_a$ for all $a\in \mathrm{A}$ and $ U\tilde\Lambda_\sigma(U^\dagger (\argdot) U)U^\dagger=\Lambda_\sigma(\argdot)$ for all $\sigma\in \Sigma$. %(up to a complex conjugation applied to all components).
\end{definition}
Note that in the characterization literature, a more general notion of gauge freedom is often discussed, but this choice seems natural in the given quantum mechanical setting.


Finally, we explain one of the main novelties of the QSQ protocol, a strong notion of certification that avoids reliance on correlations and instead tests for the occurrence of correct outcomes for every input sequence.
For a quantum model $\mathcal{M}$, we define the \emph{output map} $a_\mathcal{M}$
that assigns to each input string $w=\sigma_{i_1}\dots \sigma_{i_l}$ the set of outcomes that can be observed with non-zero probability, 
\begin{equation}
    a_\mathcal{M}(w)= \Set{a\in \mathrm{A} | \tr[M_a \Lambda_{\sigma_{i_l}}\circ\dots \circ\Lambda_{\sigma_{i_1}}(\rho)]>0}. 
\end{equation}
We use the output map instead of observed correlations to define the following stronger notion of self-testing.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:self-testing}
    Let $\mathcal{M} = (\rho,\{\Lambda_\sigma\}_{ \sigma \in \Sigma},\{M_a\}_{a\in \mathrm{A}})$ be a $d$-dimensional quantum model. 
    We say that a set of inputs $\mathcal{X}\subseteq \Sigma^*$ quasi-deterministically self-tests $\mathcal{M}$, if every $d$-dimensional model $\mathcal{N}=(\tilde\rho,\{\tilde\Lambda_{\sigma}\}_{\sigma \in \Sigma},\{\tilde M_a\}_{a\in \mathrm{A}})$ satisfying $a_\mathcal{N}(w) \subseteq a_{\mathcal{M}}(w)$ for all $w\in\mathcal{X} $  is equivalent to $\mathcal{M}$.
\end{definition}

In most cases that we discuss here, the outcome set will only contain one element; however, we require a more general treatment for multi-qubit quantum computation. 

The algorithmic procedure for self-testing quantum models is summarized in \cref{protocol}, which is called \acf{QSQ}.
In each run of the \ac{QSQ} protocol, the classical user samples an input $w$ from a suitable set of instructions $\mathcal{X}$ according to a distribution $\bm{\mu}$, sends it to the quantum computer, and gets a response $a$. 
If the response is not among the measurement outcomes expected from the target quantum model, the protocol aborts by rejecting the implemented quantum model.

{
\floatname{algorithm}{Procedure}
\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Quantum system quizzing (QSQ)}\label{protocol}
\begin{algorithmic}
\STATE \textbf{Input:} $N$ -- number of repetitions, $\mathcal{X}$ -- set of gate sequences, $a_\mathcal{M}$ -- the output map for a quantum model $\mathcal{M}$ to be certified, and $\bm{\mu}$ --  probability distribution over $\mathcal{X}$.
\FOR{$i\in \{1,\dots, N\}$}
\STATE sample $w$ from $\mathcal{X}$, according to $\bm{\mu}$;
\STATE run the quantum circuit as depicted in Fig.~\ref{fig:scenario}, record the outcome $a$;
\IF{$a\notin a_\mathcal{M}(w)$}
\STATE{\RETURN \vbt{``reject''};}
\ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\STATE \RETURN \vbt{``accept''}.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
}

Recently, it was demonstrated that a universal gate set $\{H,S,T,\mathrm{CNOT}\}$ can be certified in the~\ac{QSQ} protocol~\cite{noller2024classical,noller2024self}. We will show that all the examples of the quizzes are instances of promise problems and formalize the dimension separation between quantum and classical models. Let us first go back to the promise problem $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$.


\subsection{Gate certification from $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$}

 We establish a general connection between the DFAs solving a promise problems and the corresponding unique qubit QFAs. Note that typically, promise problems are presented in terms of languages with infinitely long words as is the case for $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$. For the practical certification protocol, however,  we later consider finite word sizes. We derive new results for finite-length languages and the minimal state complexity of a DFA that solves them. 

\begin{theorem}~\label{thm:UnaryQFAself-testing} Any qubit QFA that solves the promise problem $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$  is equivalent to the one where the starting and accepting states are $\ket{+}$, the rejecting state is $\ket{-}$, and a transformation is realized by a diagonal matrix in the computational basis, which is a generator of a faithful unitary representation of the cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}_{2^{k+1}}$ in a qubit subspace. 
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
We first prove that the only transformations allowed in QFA must be a representation, and then we show that it must be a faithful one in order to solve the promise problem. %Thus, we first need to show that $U_\sigma^{2^{k+1}} \propto \mathbbm{1}$. 

Since we know that $U_\sigma^{2^{k}}$ maps the starting state $\ket{\psi_a}$  to its orthonormal state $\ket{\psi_r}$, we know that $U_\sigma$ cannot be a rotation around the axis defined by the starting state ($\ket{\psi_a}$, $\ket{\psi_r}$ are not eigenstates of $U_\sigma$). Instead, it corresponds to a rotation matrix with an angle that is a rational multiple of $2\pi$. As a result, we have $\ket{\psi_a}\propto(U_\sigma)^{2^{k+1}} \ket{\psi_a}$ and $\ket{\psi_r}\propto(U_\sigma)^{2^{k+1}} \ket{\psi_r}$, from which it directly follows that $U_\sigma^{2^{k+1}} \propto \mathbbm{1}$, and we are working with a group representation. 

Next, we prove that it is a faithful representation. For this, we enumerate every state  $\ket{\psi_i}$, $0 \leq i< 2^{k+1}$  that the QFA realizes via the transformation $\ket{\psi_i}=(U_\sigma)^i \ket{\psi_a}$ by a classical state $s_i$ in a corresponding DFA. 
We need to show that $\left|\braket{\psi_i}{\psi_j}\right|^2\neq 1$, for $i\neq j$, $0 \leq i,j< 2^{k+1}$.

From Theorem~\ref{theorem:originalpp} and the subsequent discussion, we know that all the states $\{s_i\}$  are distinguishable and the transitions between them cannot form a shorter loop than the one with the length $2^{k+1}$ (or $l 2^{k+1}$, where $l>1$, but this case is a trivial extension of $l=1$).
If $\left|\braket{\psi_i}{\psi_j}\right|^2= 1$, for any $i\neq j$, $0 \leq i,j< 2^{k+1}$, this would result to the same state $s_i=s_j$, which would be a contradiction to the minimality of the DFA solving $\mathtt{EVENODD_k}$. Thus, the QFA forms a faithful representation of the cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}_{2^{k+1}}$ in a qubit subspace.  Since $\mathbb{Z}_{2^{k+1}}$ is abelian and finite, all its representations are also abelian and unitary (without loss of generality). In particular, the associated matrices all commute, and hence can be jointly diagonalised.
 Therefore, any representation of $\mathbb{Z}_{2^{k+1}}$ decomposes as a direct sum of one-dimensional irreducible representations. In our case we obtain two summands, so that $U_\sigma =\diag(\omega^m,\omega^n)$ with \(m, n \in \{0, 1, \dots, 2^{k+1} - 1\}\), and $\omega = e^{2\pi i / 2^{k+1}}$. 
Since the global phase does not play any role, we can choose to work with $m=0$, without loss of generality. Moreover, since the representation is faithful, $n$ cannot be an even number, and the transformation must have the form in the computational basis: 
\begin{equation}
   U_\sigma\in\{\mathrm{diag}(1, \omega^n) \ |\ n\in \{ 1,3, \dots, 2^{k+1} - 1\}\}. 
\end{equation}
As a starting state, we can then choose any pure state in the $XY$-plane, as they all correspond to the equivalent QFAs. The choice $\ket{\psi_a}=\ket{+}$ determines that $\ket{\psi_r}=\ket{-}$.
\end{proof}


A few remarks are in order here. First,~\cref{thm:UnaryQFAself-testing} serves as a subroutine of the self-testing proof of a quantum model in the QSQ protocol. The latter does not assume the QFA structure, that the states are pure, and the transitions are unitary.
Second, Theorem~\ref{thm:UnaryQFAself-testing} and the infinite promise problems identify quantum models that cannot be certified in the QSQ protocol in the strict sense.  To give an example, the promise problem $\mathtt{EVENODD}^2$, has four possible QFAs as a solution with the transition matrix $U_\sigma\in\{T, T^*, ZT, T^*Z\}$. The pairs of solutions are connected via complex conjugation, and, thus, they result in the equivalent quantum models as in~\cref{def:modelequivalence}. However, the first and the third models are not (anti-)unitary gauge equivalent, but they result in the same statistics in the QSQ experiment. Thus, they can never be distinguished from one another in the QSQ protocol. Permutations and relabelings of states in the minimal DFA that solves the promise problem $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$ enumerate these inequivalences. See Fig.~\ref{fig:tautomaton} for an example for $k=2$.

\section{Quantum-inspired Promise Problems beyond Unary Qubit Settings}

From the succinctness result in the unary promise problems $\mathtt{EVENODD_k}$, we can make the following observation. In $\mathtt{EVENODD_k}$, the promise problem is solved if we can correctly count the number of applied transitions per input sequence. We need to differentiate between odd and even multiples of $2^{k}$. For \acp{QFA}, a single qubit suffices to solve the promise problem because the intermediate states, the states traversed during operations $U_{\sigma}^k \ket{+}$, required for correctly counting the applied gate, exist only implicitly. However, for the ``smallest'' \ac{DFA} solution, i.e., the \ac{DFA} with the fewest states solving the promise problem, these ``hidden'' intermediate states enabling the counting task must exist explicitly as part of the \ac{DFA} states. Since both, \ac{QFA} and \ac{DFA} implement the same task, the \ac{DFA} must be equivalent if we include these ``hidden'' intermediate states in the picture, i.e., the transition graph spanned in the Bloch sphere (cf. Fig.~\ref{fig:sautomata}(a), Fig.~\ref{fig:tautomaton}(a) or Fig.~\ref{fig:qfatodfa}) generated by the gate $\sqrt[2^k]{Z}$ and the initial state $\ket{+}$. We used this equivalence to map the DFA solution to a QFA, which manifests the equivalence with a specific quantum model (Theorem \ref{thm:UnaryQFAself-testing}).
Motivated by this observation, we ask whether things work the other way around: can we define a promise problem from specific quantum gates and initial states using well-established tools and arguments from automata theory?

We first attempt a clean procedure for defining a \ac{DFA} from specific quantum gates and an initial state. In the following, we focus on \textit{finite groups} generated from certain qubit gates.

\begin{definition} \label{def:dfafromqfa} (Defining a DFA from a finite group QFA)
 Let $\mathcal{M}$ be a QFA defined via the initial state $\ket{0}$, which is also the $\mathtt{accept}$ state, the $\mathtt{reject}$  state given by the orthonormal $\ket{r}$, $\bra{0}\ket{r} = 0$, and unitary gates $U_{\sigma_i}$, $\sigma_i \in \Sigma$, which are generators of a finite group. Then, we can construct a DFA from this QFA by associating the $\mathtt{accept}$ and $\mathtt{reject}$  states, as well as the intermediate states during gate application, as states in the DFA sense. More concretely, we define from such a QFA $\mathcal{M}$ a DFA $\mathcal{A} = (S, \Sigma, \delta, s_0, S_{yes}, S_{no})$, where $s_0 \equiv \ket{0}$, $S_{yes} = { s_0 }$, $s_r \equiv \ket{r}$, $S_{no} = { s_r }$, and non-final states $s_i \in S$, where each $s_i$ is associated with a state generated by the group elements unique up to global phases and the unitary gauge. Since we assume finite groups, we obtain a finite graph. The transition function $\delta$ documents which generator maps the current state to which other state.
\end{definition}

We note several points: the automaton is defined via a group. That is, for every word $w \in \Sigma$ and every state $s$, there exists exactly one state $s'$ such that $\delta(s',w)=s$, so $\delta$ defines a complete one-to-one function. Such languages are called group languages, and have been defined as languages that can be recognized via a finite group, i.e., a group automaton, or also called permutation automaton, having a transition function as described above \cite{thierrin1968permutation,place2022group}. Additionally, it is known that the languages a \ac{QFA} can recognize are exactly the group languages \cite{brodsky1999characterizations}. We could have alternatively started directly from the Cayley graph of the generators of the quantum model to define the DFA, associating each node with a DFA state and deriving transitions accordingly. However, in our setting, the interplay with initial states is crucial: we might choose initial states that cause the Cayley graph to contain the same state repeatedly. Although this would not alter the fundamental result, we opt to begin with the most compact DFA representation.

For the sake of completeness, we document notations for defining a \ac{DFA} from a Cayley graph as follows.

\begin{definition} (Cayley Graph from \cite{cayley1878desiderata})
     A Cayley graph $C(G,X)$ is a directed graph defined from a group $G$ and its generator $X$. Vertices of $C$ are the elements $g \in G$ and edges depict transitions given by the generators $G \times X \rightarrow G$.
\end{definition}

We illustrate the \ac{DFA} construction through an example. If we label the initial element as $\ket{+}$ and choose $X = \{ \sqrt[2^k]{Z} \}$, the resulting Cayley graph is a cyclic graph of length $2^{k+1}$. Introducing $\mathtt{accept}$ and $\mathtt{reject}$  states $G_{yes}$ and $G_{no}$ transforms this Cayley graph into a DFA $\mathcal{C} = (S \equiv G, \Sigma \equiv X, \delta \equiv G \times X \rightarrow G, s_0 \equiv \ket{+}, S_{yes} \equiv G_{yes}, S_{no} \equiv G_{no})$. The transition function $\delta(s_k,w)$ becomes equivalent to $w^{\text{reversed}}\ket{s_k}$, where the superscript indicates reversed gate order to preserve left-to-right reading intuition. Henceforth, we omit this superscript but implicitly assume the reversed order when applying a word to a state.

We move next to how to define a promise problem from a given DFA.

\begin{definition}\label{def:dfainducedpp} (DFA-induced promise problem) Let $\mathcal{A}$ be a DFA. Then a DFA-induced promise problem is given as $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A}) = (\mathtt{PP}_{yes}(\mathcal{A}), \mathtt{PP}_{no}(\mathcal{A}))$, where the languages, the yes- and the no-language are induced by $\mathcal{A}$
    \begin{equation}
        % \mathtt{PP}_{yes}(\mathcal{A}) = \{ w | w \in \Sigma^* , \delta(s_0,w) \in S_{yes}\},
        \mathtt{PP}_{yes}(\mathcal{A}) = \bigcup_{s \in S_{yes}} \mathcal{L}(s) ,
    \end{equation}
    and the no-language
    \begin{equation}
 %       \mathtt{PP}_{no}(\mathcal{A}) = \{ w | w \in \Sigma^* , \delta(s_0,w) \in S_{no}\}.
         \mathtt{PP}_{no}(\mathcal{A}) = \bigcup_{s \in S_{no}} \mathcal{L}(s) ,
    \end{equation}
    where $\mathcal{L}(s) = \{ w \in \Sigma^* | \delta(s_0,w) = s \}$ .
\end{definition}

We state an apparent observation for the sake of completeness.

\begin{theorem}
    Let $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A})$ be a promise problem induced from a DFA $\mathcal{A}$ constructed from a qubit ($|Q| = 2$) QFA $\mathcal{M}$.  Then, $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A})$ is solved by the same QFA $\mathcal{M}$ with dimension $|Q| = 2$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
     The QFA $\mathcal{M}$ and the DFA $\mathcal{A}$ constructed from $\mathcal{M}$ share the same $\mathtt{accept}$ and $\mathtt{reject}$  states, thus the words in $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A})$ map to the corresponding states, which solves the promise problem. In $\mathcal{M}$, the intermediate states exist as a superposition of the $\mathtt{accept}$ and $\mathtt{reject}$  states, which does not require additional states beyond these final states to resolve. Thus, $\mathcal{M}$ has dimension $|Q| = 2$.
\end{proof}


What is the state complexity of a DFA that solves such a promise problem? The trivial solution in the classical setting is given by the promise problem-inducing \ac{DFA}—this would certainly solve the promise problem. However, is it minimal?

The answer to this question is closely tied to the DFA minimization problem, which has been studied since the early days of automata theory. This field concerns the equivalence of automata and minimal instances among equivalent \acp{DFA}. We review these concepts by summarizing key results from Ref.~\cite{hopcroft2001introduction}.

Before discussing the equivalence of two DFAs, we first examine the equivalence of states within the same \ac{DFA}.

\begin{definition}\label{def:equivstate} (Equivalence of two states, from \cite{hopcroft2001introduction}) 
    Two states of a given \ac{DFA} $\mathcal{A}$, $s_i, s_j \in S$ are considered equivalent, if both, $s_i$ and $s_j$, are accepting or both rejecting. For equivalent states, this shall also be given for all $w \in \Sigma^*$, $\delta(s_i,w)$, and $\delta(s_j,w)$. No word $w$ should map the pair $\delta(s_i,w)$ and $\delta(s_j,w)$ to a pair of one $\mathtt{accept}$ and one $\mathtt{reject}$  state. Then, we say  $s_i, s_j$ are \textit{indistinguishable} and write $s_i =_{\mathcal{A}} s_j$. If there is a $w \in \Sigma^*$ that maps $s_i, s_j$ to differently labeled states, we say  $s_i, s_j$ are \textit{distinguishable} and write $s_i \neq_{\mathcal{A} }s_j$. 
\end{definition}

Ref.~\cite{hopcroft2001introduction} also introduced an algorithm to compute all distinguishable pairs within a \ac{DFA}, called the \textit{table-filling algorithm}. Here, \ac{DFA} refers to the regular DFA with two kinds of states, i.e., $\mathtt{accept}$ and $\mathtt{reject}$. The algorithm works in two steps: it first marks all the distinguishable state pairs and then sweeps through the set of all input pairs whose transition leads to states that are already marked as distinguishable. 

\begin{algorithm}[H]

\caption{Table-filling~(adopted from~\cite{hopcroft2001introduction})}\label{algo:tablefilling}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    
\REQUIRE DFA $\mathcal{A} = (S, \Sigma, \delta, s_0, S_a)$
\ENSURE Table $T$ marking all distinguishable state pairs
\STATE Initialize table $T$ with all unique pairs of states from $S$
\FOR{pair $(p,q)$ in $T$}
    \IF{$(p \in S_a \land q \in S \setminus S_a) \lor (q \in S_a \land p \in S \setminus S_a)$}
        \STATE Mark $T[p,q]$ with $\mathtt{x}$
    \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\REPEAT
    \STATE $\text{changed} \leftarrow \text{false}$
    \FOR{ all pairs $(p,q)$ marked with $\mathtt{x}$}
        \FOR{all letters $\sigma \in \Sigma$}
            \FOR{all pairs $(r,s)$ such that $\delta(r,\sigma) = p$ and $\delta(s,\sigma) = q$}
                \IF{$T[r,s]$ is not marked with $\mathtt{x}$}
                    \STATE Mark $T[r,s]$ with $\mathtt{x}$
                    \STATE $\text{changed} \leftarrow \text{true}$
                \ENDIF
            \ENDFOR
        \ENDFOR
    \ENDFOR
\UNTIL{$\text{changed} = \text{false}$}
\STATE \RETURN table $T$

    \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


Note that the input \ac{DFA} is not a promise version \ac{DFA}, i.e., all non-accepting states are assumed to be rejecting states. In words, Algorithm~\ref{algo:tablefilling} operates as follows. We first mark the states that can be distinguished by the empty word. Then we mark every pair that maps to an already marked pair via a single letter transition, since they have a word that demonstrates distinguishability. This procedure is repeated until no new marks can be placed. This procedure is summarized in an example for the 6-cycle $\mathcal{C}_6$ in Fig.~\ref{fig:min6}.

Theorem 4.20 in Ref.~\cite{hopcroft2001introduction} proves that pairs not marked by the table-filling algorithm are indistinguishable.


From this table, we can next deduce the minimal \ac{DFA}, i.e., the smallest equivalent \ac{DFA} to $\mathtt{accept}$ the same language -- which happens to be the unique automaton up to relabeling of the states. 

\begin{definition} (Minimal DFA from Ref.~\cite{hopcroft2001introduction})
 A DFA is said to be \textit{minimal} if the following conditions are fulfilled:
\begin{itemize}
    \item all states are reachable from the initial state $s_0$
    \item the DFA has the minimum number of states among all DFAs accepting the same language
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}


\begin{algorithm}
\caption{DFA Minimization alias Hopcroft's Algorithm (adopted from Ref.~\cite{hopcroft2001introduction})}
\label{alg:dfamin}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE DFA $\mathcal{A} = (S, \Sigma, \delta, s_0, S_a)$
\ENSURE Minimal DFA $\mathcal{A}_{min}$
\STATE Eliminate any states which cannot be reached from the starting state $s_0$
\STATE Run the table-filling algorithm to identify distinguishable state pairs
\STATE Group equivalent states into blocks:
\STATE \quad Create equivalence classes from unmarked pairs (i.e., $(p,q)$ unmarked $p =_{\mathcal{A}} q$) 
\STATE \quad marked pairs indicate states not belonging to the same equivalence class
\FOR{each block of states}
    \STATE Merge all states of a block into a single representative state, resulting in $S' \subseteq S$
\ENDFOR
\STATE Update the transition function $\delta \rightarrow \delta'$ to reflect the merged states
\STATE \RETURN minimal DFA $\mathcal{A}_{min} = (S', \Sigma, \delta', s_0, S_a')$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Note that merging states is unproblematic because states within a block are indistinguishable, i.e., all inputs map to the same output. The transitions also merge without conflicts: assume two states $p,q$ belonging to the same block $B$ have a letter that maps them to different blocks. Then we would have mapped $p,q$ to distinguishable states by definition, making them distinguishable and causing a contradiction.

Moreover, Theorem 4.26 in Ref.~\cite{hopcroft2001introduction} shows that a \ac{DFA} constructed this way is minimal and unique up to relabeling of states.

\begin{theorem}\label{thr:minimaldfa} (Theorem 4.26 in Ref.~\cite{hopcroft2001introduction})  A DFA that has undergone Hopcroft's  Algorithm (Algorithm~\ref{alg:dfamin}) is the smallest DFA to recognize a certain language and is unique up to relabeling of states.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof} (Paraphrased from Ref.~\cite{hopcroft2001introduction}) Assume a DFA $\mathcal{B}$ with fewer states than a DFA $\mathcal{A}$ that accepts the same language $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{A})$, where $\mathcal{A}$ is minimal. We define the union DFA of these two DFAs, i.e., a DFA containing both $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$ with no transitions between them and state labels inherited from each DFA. We can then check pairwise distinguishability: the initial states $s_0^{\mathcal{B}}$ and $s_0^{\mathcal{A}}$ must be indistinguishable since both shall accept the same language. The same holds for all pairs obtained via any $w \in \Sigma^*$: $\delta(s_0^{\mathcal{B}},w)$ and $\delta(s_0^{\mathcal{A}},w)$. Since $\mathcal{A}$ has more states, by the pigeonhole principle, there exist two states in $\mathcal{A}$ that are indistinguishable from a single state in $\mathcal{B}$. This contradicts our assumption that $\mathcal{A}$ is minimal.
\end{proof}

In terms of complexity, we note that the Hopcroft method, as presented above, requires examining all pairs $\binom{n}{2} \approx n^2$ for at most $n^2$ rounds, resulting in a $\mathcal{O}(n^4)$ scaling in the worst case. Since this method is intuitive and easy to grasp, we retain it for now. However, the authors in Ref.~\cite{hopcroft2001introduction} already mention a method to reduce the time complexity to $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ and even $\mathcal{O}(n \log n)$ \cite{knuutila2001re, berstel2010minimization}, via a more efficient table-filling method, which is still known to be the fastest algorithm for \ac{DFA} minimization.

Having carefully introduced the notion of minimal \ac{DFA}, we can establish results regarding DFA-induced promise problems from direct implications of minimality. Before doing so, we need to extend minimality to \acp{pvDFA} -- here we have instead of two, three categories or labels of states: $\mathtt{accept}$, $\mathtt{reject}$, and $\mathtt{indefinite}$. We only need to modify Definition~\ref{def:equivstate}.

\begin{definition}
    \label{def:equivstatepv}
    (Equivalence of two states in a pvDFA, modified from Def.~\ref{def:equivstate}) Two states of a given \ac{pvDFA} $\mathcal{A}$, $s_i,s_j\in S$, $i \neq j$ are considered equivalent or indistinguishable if for all $w \in \Sigma^*$, $\delta(s_i,w)$ and $\delta(s_j,w)$ belong to the same category, i.e. $\mathtt{accept}$, $\mathtt{reject}$  or $\mathtt{indefinite}$. Otherwise,  $s_i,s_j$ are distinguishable.
\end{definition}

If dealing with \acp{pvDFA}, we only have to replace the notion of state equivalence in Algorithms \ref{algo:tablefilling} and \ref{alg:dfamin} with Def.~\ref{def:equivstatepv} to adapt to \acp{pvDFA}. 

This allows us to connect minimality to minimal solutions of DFA-induced promise problems.

\begin{proposition}
    If the promise problem $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A})$ is induced from a minimal automaton, $\mathcal{A}$ is the smallest automaton to recognize this promise problem.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    When we defined the \ac{DFA}-induced promise problem in Def.~\ref{def:dfainducedpp}, we defined the yes-language as the language accepted by the \ac{DFA} $\mathcal{A}$. By Theorem \ref{thr:minimaldfa}, if $\mathcal{A}$ is minimal, there is no smaller \ac{DFA} recognizing the yes-language. The same holds for the no-language, which is the language rejected by $\mathcal{A}$ and the ``indefinite language'', defined by the states that neither $\mathtt{accept}$ nor $\mathtt{reject}$. If any states required to describe these languages were indistinguishable, $\mathcal{A}$ was not minimal. Hence, $\mathcal{A}$ is the smallest \ac{DFA} to recognize the promise problem.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Minimal solution to the DFA-induced PP in solvation mode}
While arguing minimal solutions to the promise problem in recognition mode is somewhat trivial, we observe subtleties when studying solutions to DFA-induced promise problems in solvation mode—the more relevant scenario for gate certification applications. It is easy to see that a minimal automaton in the Hopcroft sense is inadequate for arguing the minimality of promise problems in solvation mode. Consider a cyclic graph of length 6, $\mathcal{C}_6$, with the initial state as accepting and the third state as rejecting. This induces the promise problem $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{C}_6) = (\{0,6,12,\dots \},\{3,9,15,\dots\})$ (cf. Fig.~\ref{fig:min6}). We argue the minimality of the language-defining DFAs, starting with the yes-language. In the yes-language, only the initial state is accepting. All non-accepting states are distinguishable from the initial state and from each other because it is always possible to find a word mapping one of the investigated state pairs to the initial state in a cyclic graph. Hence, such a DFA would be minimal in recognition mode. However, from our previous intuitions, we know this unary promise problem is solvable with just two states since a $\bmod 2$ operation alone suffices to differentiate between even and odd input lengths; thus, the yes- and no-languages. 

The fallacy was demanding minimality in recognition mode -- while the $\mathcal{C}_6$ automaton is indeed minimal when producing exactly this language, the promise problem in solvation mode poses a different question. In solvation mode, since we restrict the input given to the automaton to the promise languages, it is never required that the automaton accepts or rejects  \textit{exclusively} the promise languages. An automaton $\mathcal{A}$ that includes the promise language as a subset of its accepted language $\mathtt{PP_{yes}} \subseteq \mathcal{L}(\mathcal{A})$ will solve the promise problem if the accepted language does not overlap with the promised no-language $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{A}) \cup \mathtt{PP_{no}} = \emptyset$. Thus, the actual question when seeking the solution automaton in solvation mode is: 
\begin{displayquote}
    "What is the minimal automaton that contains the yes-language as a subset of the accepted language while avoiding any intersection with the no-language?" 
\end{displayquote}

In this context, assigning $\mathtt{accept}$ or $\mathtt{reject}$  labels to $\mathtt{indefinite}$ states becomes crucial. When we defined the promise problem from a \ac{DFA} $\mathcal{A}$, we implicitly partitioned the space of all words $\Sigma^*$ into three parts: the yes-language consisting of words mapping the initial state to any accepting state, the no-language consisting of words mapping the initial state to any rejecting state, and the remaining $\mathtt{indefinite}$ language consisting of words mapping the initial state to any $\mathtt{indefinite}$ state. 

It follows that $\mathcal{A}$ solves $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A})$, as does any $\mathtt{accept}$/$\mathtt{reject}$  assignment of the $\mathtt{indefinite}$ states in $\mathcal{A}$: although the set of accepted words of such modified $\mathcal{A}$ is now larger than the original yes-language, we never check words beyond the yes-language. Therefore, if any $\mathtt{accept}$/$\mathtt{reject}$  assignment of $\mathtt{indefinite}$ states in $\mathcal{A}$ is non-minimal, then the minimized version -- which we call the \textit{minimal automaton in solvation mode}  -- is a smaller solution to the promise problem.

This requires examining all possible state assignments for potential minimization. Returning to our cycle length 6 example, an alternating $\mathtt{accept}$-$\mathtt{reject}$  pattern starting from the initial state renders all $\mathtt{accept}$ states indistinguishable from each other, and likewise for $\mathtt{reject}$  states. This allows reduction to a two-state DFA, matching the state complexity derived from modular arithmetic arguments. 
While minimization is generally an efficient algorithm, running this for all $2^{\text{$\#$ $\mathtt{indefinite}$ states}}$ combinations becomes infeasible for larger systems. Fortunately, many promise problem classes like the unary $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$ allow for determining minimal complexity through number-theoretic arguments, bypassing this algorithmic strategy. However, we advocate this reasoning for more ``exotic'' quantum-inspired promise problems, such as those generated from non-commuting gate sets, e.g., $X = \{s,h \}$ discussed later.

Although it does not change the intractability of the problem itself, we can modify Algorithm~\ref{alg:dfamin} to account for arbitrary assignments of the $\mathtt{indefinite}$ states by casting the equivalence assignments into a satisfiability problem, where we have access to numerous numerical tools to tackle this with commodity SAT solvers. We illustrate this modification in the following in further detail.

In the table-filling Algorithm (Algorithm~\ref{algo:tablefilling}), the goal is to check step-by-step whether pairs of states can be mapped to distinguishable pairs via the same word. With many $\mathtt{indefinite}$ states -- as in typical DFA-induced promise problems where only one state accepts and one rejects -- we cannot fill the table for most nodes, not because they are indistinguishable, but because we cannot definitively rule out that possibility. However, we can define clusters of pairs, i.e., pairs of states that map to each other. If any state pairs are distinguishable or indistinguishable, all other pairs within the same cluster are too.

We refer to the algorithm that performs the clustering of pairs as the promise-version Hopcroft algorithm. The core modification lies in the table-filling; we inductively consider all pairs and mark them with $\mathtt{x}$, but we use different colors depending on their cluster.

We assign a variable $l_i$ to each state $s_i$, indicating its label, e.g., $l_i = 0$ if state $s_i$ is accepting and $l_i = 1$ if state $s_i$ is rejecting. From the DFA $\mathcal{A}$ that defines the promise problem, we know pairs that are definitely distinguishable. Consider the $\mathtt{accept}$ state $s_a$, which is typically chosen to be the initial state, and a $\mathtt{reject}$  state $s_r$ that defines the no-language. These obey an inequality condition $l_a \overset{!}{\neq} l_r$. Pairs within the same cluster as $(s_a,s_r)$, so all pairs reachable with any word $w \in \Sigma^*$, $(\delta(s_a, w), \delta(s_r, w))$ also obey such inequality conditions. Since we marked them in the original Hopcroft algorithm with a black $\mathtt{x}$, we call this the $\mathtt{x}$-cluster. 
In the original Hopcroft algorithm, an unmarked pair $(s_i,s_j)$ is indistinguishable and could be merged, i.e., $l_i = l_j$ can be satisfied without contradicting any inequality constraints demanded from the distinguishable pairs. 

In this promise-version algorithm, we have a substantial number of $\mathtt{indefinite}$ states, which are neither accepting nor rejecting; in other words, the purpose of the promise-version algorithm is to find the $\mathtt{accept}$ and $\mathtt{reject}$  assignments of the $\mathtt{indefinite}$ states, such that we obtain the highest count of mergable states, resulting then in the minimal automaton to solve the promise problem in solvation mode. 
Hence, in the promise-version Hopcroft algorithm, we identify all unmarked pairs with their own cluster characterising pairs that map within each other. To contrast these additional clusters from the $\mathtt{x}$-cluster indicating distinguishable pairs prompting inequality conditions, we associate the cluster resulting from indefinite pairs with different colors, see Fig.~\ref{fig:minsolv6}. 
The automaton can be minimized if all states within one cluster can have pairwise identical labels, i.e., obey pair-wise equality relations $l_p = l_q$ for all states $s_p, s_q$ within the same cluster without violating the inequality relations demanded via the $\mathtt{x}$-cluster.
To find the minimal automaton, we must first check if the equality relations from a single non-$\mathtt{x}$-cluster are compatible with the inequality conditions from the $\mathtt{x}$-cluster. As a second step, we must test the equality conditions from multiple non-$\mathtt{x}$-cluster for mutual satisfiability. Finally, we shall rank the equality relations to be fulfilled such that we obtain the set of inequality and equality constraints that maximize the number of fulfilled equality constraints and minimize the automaton.

We summarize the above procedure in the following pseudo-code, and name it the promise-version Hopcroft algorithm for DFA minimization in solvation mode. An illustration of the procedure is given in Fig.~\ref{fig:minsolv6} for the $\mathcal{C}_6$ automaton.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Promise-version Hopcroft Algorithm}
\label{alg:mindfasolv}
\begin{algorithmic}

\REQUIRE DFA $\mathcal{A} = (S, \Sigma, \delta, s_0, S_a, S_r)$ with seed pair $(s_a, s_r)$
\ENSURE Minimal DFA in solvation mode
\STATE Step 1: Preprocessing
\STATE Eliminate any states that cannot be reached from the starting state
\STATE Generate a table $T$ containing all unique pairs of states from $S$
\STATE Step 2: Find pairs within the same cluster
\STATE Place an $\mathtt{x}$ between $s_a$ and $s_r$, our seed pair
\STATE cluster marked with black $\mathtt{x}$ is called $\mathtt{x}$-cluster
\REPEAT
    \STATE $\text{changed} \leftarrow \text{false}$
    \FOR{ all pairs $(p,q)$ marked with $\mathtt{x}$}
        \FOR{all letters $\sigma \in \Sigma$}
            \FOR{all pairs $(r,s)$ such that $\delta(r,\sigma) = p$ and $\delta(s,\sigma) = q$}
                \IF{$T[r,s]$ is not marked with $\mathtt{x}$}
                    \STATE Mark $T[r,s]$ with $\mathtt{x}$
                    \STATE $\text{changed} \leftarrow \text{true}$
                \ENDIF
            \ENDFOR
        \ENDFOR
    \ENDFOR
\UNTIL{$\text{changed} = \text{false}$}

\STATE Step 3: Repeat Step 2 with different colors until all pairs are assigned
\WHILE{there are unmarked pairs left}
    \STATE Pick one unmarked pair as the new seed pair
    \STATE Repeat Step 2, but use a different color for marks
    \STATE Each color defines a cluster
    \IF{during this step, we encounter a marked pair with a different color}
        \STATE replace all marked pairs with that color
    \ENDIF
\ENDWHILE

\STATE Step 4: check satisfiability for possible state merging
\FOR{each cluster}
    \STATE Compute satisfiability equivalence relation from cluster and inequality relation from  $\mathtt{x}$-cluster
    \IF{satisfiable}
        \STATE Group equivalent states into blocks
    \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\FOR{all combinations of clusters compatible with inequality relations}
    \STATE check mutual satisfiability
\ENDFOR
\STATE Step 5: Select the cluster or combination of clusters that leads to the smallest number of blocks
\STATE Merge states according to these blocks
\STATE Update transition function accordingly
\STATE \RETURN minimal automaton

\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:minsolv6}, Algorithm \ref{alg:mindfasolv} successfully identifies the correct minimal solution for the $\mathcal{C}_6$ promise problem in solvation mode. For this example, we can confirm that no smaller DFA exists to solve the promise problem, as at least two states are required.

From this example, we can also infer the effect of the promise-version Hopcroft algorithm on the processed automaton. Merging two states is only allowed if these states are indistinguishable, i.e., no word would map these states to differently labeled states. For automata defined from finite groups, this is only possible if these states can be associated with the same element in a subgroup of the group underlying the automaton. Also, needless to say, these states associated with the same element of the subgroup have to be of the same type (i.e., $\mathtt{accept}$ or reject). Let us examine that statement in the case of the $\mathcal{C}_6$ example, which is equivalent to the $\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z}_6$ Cayley graph. According to Lagrange's theorem, subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z}_6$ are $\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z}_2$ and $\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z}_3$. To merge $\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z}_6$ into a $\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z}_3$ graph, every third state must belong to the same label -- this cannot be achieved since the initial state and the $\mathtt{reject}$  state are also three states away. However, $\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z}_2$ is compatible, and that is why the promise-version Hopcroft algorithm outputs a two-state solution.

Can we, however, guarantee that no other automaton -- possibly not generated via minimizing the promise problem inducing \ac{DFA} -- solves the promise problem? We argue affirmatively for DFA-induced promise problems when the DFA describes a group language, which occurs when defining a DFA from a QFA. For more general cases, we identify where our group language argumentation fails but leave this as a conjecture.

Let us start with a restrictive choice of a group-language DFA (that is, a DFA defined from a finite group):
\begin{proposition}\label{prop:minsolvation}
    The DFA solving a promise problem $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A})$, is given by the minimal automaton of $\mathcal{A}$ in solvation mode computed with Algorithm \ref{alg:mindfasolv}. We assume the case where $\mathcal{A}$ is a finite-group automaton and has one $\mathtt{accept}$ state $s_0$, which is also the initial state, and one $\mathtt{reject}$  state $s_r$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    We prove optimality in two steps: first, we show that minimizing $\mathcal{A}$ in solvation mode yields a solution to $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A})$; second, we demonstrate that no smaller DFA can solve $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A})$.

    Without loss of generality,  we can assume $\mathcal{A}$ is minimal for recognition. If not, we apply the standard minimization procedure beforehand.

    The promise-version Hopcroft algorithm seeks a minimal DFA among all possible $\mathtt{accept}$/$\mathtt{reject}$  assignments of $\mathtt{indefinite}$ states in $\mathcal{A}$. The indistinguishability of the promise problem's defining states, $s_0$ (initial state) defining the yes-language, and the no-language defining $\mathtt{reject}$  state $s_r$, is preserved since these states remain unmodified. Since the $\mathtt{accept}$ and $\mathtt{reject}$  sets satisfy the promise constraint, the minimized automaton solves the promise problem.
    
    Next, we argue that no smaller automaton can solve $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A})$. Let $\mathcal{A}'$ denote the output of the promise-version Hopcroft algorithm, and assume an automaton $\mathcal{B}$ with fewer states than $\mathcal{A}'$ solving $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A})$.

 %   Since $\mathcal{A}$ is a group language automaton with bijective transitions corresponding to a finite group, define 
    Define the languages $\mathcal{L}(b_i) = \{w \in \Sigma^* | \delta(b_0, w) = b_i \}$ for any state $b_i$ in $\mathcal{B}$. Since the empty word belongs to the yes-language, $\mathcal{B}$'s initial state must be accepting. We assume $b_0$ is not a tail state, i.e., $\mathcal{L}(b_0)$ contains infinitely many words. If not, we call the next closest non-tail $\mathtt{accept}$ state $b_0$. Such a state must exist, since $\mathcal{B}$ needs to at least accept infinite $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{A})$.
    We next make use of the fact that $\mathcal{A}$ is a finite-group automaton by considering the ``order'' of a word. We say a word $w$ is order-$k$, if $\delta(s_0,w^k)=s_0$ and no smaller $k$ would fulfill the equation.
    In $\mathcal{A}$, the yes-language $\mathcal{L}(a_0)$ contains words which are strictly ``order-1 elements'', since any word $w \in \mathcal{L}(a_0)$ maps back to the initial state $\delta(s_0, w) = s_0$, while $\mathcal{L}(a_i)$ contains words of higher orders, $w \in \mathcal{L}(a_i)$, $i \neq 0$, $\delta(s_0, w) \neq s_0$, but $\delta(s_0, w^n) = s_0$ for some $n \neq 1$. The $\mathtt{accept}$ language of $\mathcal{B}$, $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{B})$, however, may contain beyond order-1 words of $\mathcal{A}$ since solvation mode permits $\mathcal{L}(a_0) \subseteq \mathcal{L}(\mathcal{B})$.
    
    Since any repetition of $w \in \mathcal{L}(b_0)$ remains in $ w^k \in \mathcal{L}(b_0)$, the language $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{B})$ cannot overlap with $\mathcal{L}(a_i)$ for ``generating orders'' of $\mathcal{A}$. Any such overlap would allow repeated application to reach every state in $\mathcal{A}$, including the $\mathtt{reject}$  state, contradicting the assumption $\mathcal{B}$ being a valid promise problem solution.
    However, overlap with non-generating orders is allowed, as these correspond precisely to subgroups of $\mathcal{A}$. Therefore, any promise problem solution must be either $\mathcal{A}$ itself or a subgroup of $\mathcal{A}$ -- exactly what our minimization algorithm produces.
    The minimization algorithm merges only compatible states, effectively defining subgroups of $\mathcal{A}$. It identifies equivalent generator orders, then solves a satisfiability problem, ensuring $a_0$ and $a_r$ remain distinguishable. This excludes orders prohibited by $\mathcal{A}$'s labeling.
    Conclusively, the minimal solution to $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A})$ is the minimal-order subgroup of $\mathcal{A}$ not prohibited by $a_r$'s choice. Since our algorithm produces this exact subgroup, no smaller automaton exists. 
\end{proof}

The above proof does not hold for arbitrary DFAs, since we cannot apply group-theoretic arguments in general.

We note here that this kind of promise problem has been discussed under different terminology, and without any remark on quantum finite-state automata, under the name of "separation problem" in Ref.~\cite{place2022group}, where they showed explicitly for the class of group languages that the question whether, given two languages of the same graph, a third language within the same class exists that accepts the first and rejects the second, is decidable. This resembles exactly the setting of promise problems, especially with the restriction to group languages matching the quantum-inspired setting. Ref.~\cite{place2022group} stresses that this result has been shown previously also in the realm of abstract algebra \cite{ash1991inevitable}. In our work, we do not make any complexity-theoretic claim; we rather use the (potentially untractable, but decidable) minimization algorithm to discuss and comprehend the dimensional separation of promise problems better. Having developed an intuition for cases where group automata can be minimized, which is when all states associated with the same subgroup element have the same $\mathtt{accept}$ or $\mathtt{reject}$  label, we can apply this intuition to propose a constructive method to exclude certain promise problems from being valid QSQ protocols.

For instance, it becomes evident why the gate $\sqrt[3]{Z}$ cannot be certifiedted within QSQ; as already mentioned, $\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z}_6$ has two subgroups: $\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z}_2$ and $\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z}_3$. In the quantum-inspired examples, we assume that the gate to be investigated is some root of the $Z$ gate, which itself has order two, $Z^2 = \mathbbm{1}$. Measurements are demanded to be deterministic; thus, we construct our input sequences to result in either even or odd counts of $Z$. In other terms, in the unary promise problem, we defined from an order-two gate, i.e., the $Z$ gate, the root of order $k$ of the order-two gate, to define a group structure with order $2 \times k$. Since it is demanded that the initial state and the $k$-th state are distinguishable (associated with different outputs), the order-$k$ subgroup is prohibited from solving the promise problem. If 2 appears as a prime factor in $k$ with multiplicity $m$, that means also that all subgroups with order $k / 2^{m-1}$ for $m = 1, \ldots, m$ cannot be solutions to the promise problem.
That means that we exclude, through the $\mathtt{accept}$/$\mathtt{reject}$  labelings of states, that certain subgroups cannot have generated the outputs demanded by the promise problem. This intuition allows us to formulate a necessary condition for valid QSQ protocols.

\begin{theorem}
    A DFA-induced promise problem $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A})$ for Quantum System Quizzing needs to be defined from a DFA $\mathcal{A}$ that is minimal in solvation mode with more than two states. 
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    This follows directly from Proposition \ref{prop:minsolvation}. Since the minimized $\mathcal{A}$ in solvation mode is a solution to $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A})$, too, if $\mathtt{PP}(\mathcal{A})$ was not defined from an already minimal automaton, we obtain two valid group automata solving the promise problem. Since these can be associated back to QFAs, yielding at least two different gates compatible with the demanded outputs, we cannot uniquely identify the underlying quantum model. Therefore, minimality in solvation mode is a necessary condition for a valid QSQ protocol.
    QSQ relies on a dimensional separation assumption between potential quantum and classical systems. If the classical DFA requires only dimension two, this separation assumption is not fulfilled.
\end{proof}

We are now equipped with a criterion to propose potential QSQ protocols from gates generating finite groups. With this new tool at hand, we expand our investigation to the non-unary setting, where we discuss promise problems with both commuting and non-commuting gates or letters, and later extend to more general settings of multi-qubit unary problems and qudit systems.


\subsection{Non-unary promise problems with commuting letters: $\mathtt{DIOF}$}

So-called commuting languages, where permutations of letters within a word do not change the output of the associated DFA, have already been explored in the literature, e.g., in Ref.~\cite{bianchi2014complexity}, since they extend naturally from the unary $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$. In $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$, the outputs of the \ac{DFA} depend on the entire word length. In commuting languages, the output per word depends on the counts of each letter -- the ordering of letters does not matter. This property is captured by Diophantine equations:

The $s$ transition is weighted by factor $2$ compared to $t$, which solves the given $\mathtt{DIOF^{a,k}}$. \begin{definition}\label{def:diof} (modified from~\cite{bianchi2014complexity})
    The promise problem $\mathtt{DIOF^{a,k}}=(\mathtt{DIOF^{a,k}_{yes}},\mathtt{DIOF^{a,k}_{no}})$ comprises following sets of multi-letter input strings
    \begin{align}
       \mathtt{DIOF^{a,k}_{yes}} &= \{ w \in \Sigma^* | (a_1|w|_{\sigma_1} + a_2|w|_{\sigma_2} + \dots + a_H|w|_{\sigma_H}) \equiv 0 \bmod{2^{k+1}}\} \\
        \mathtt{DIOF^{a,k}_{no}} &= \{ w \in \Sigma^* | (a_1|w|_{\sigma_1} + a_2|w|_{\sigma_2} + \dots + a_H|w|_{\sigma_H}) \equiv 2^k \bmod{2^{k+1}}\},
    \end{align}
    where the alphabet consists of $H$ letters $\Sigma = (\sigma_1, \dots, \sigma_H)$ with  associated weights $\mathtt{a} = (a_1, \dots, a_H)$. The weights are given in ascending order.
    The count of the letter per word is indicated by $|\cdot|_{a_i}$. Promised input sequences are solutions to some Diophantine equations.
\end{definition}

Note that the modification compared to the original $\mathtt{DIOF}$ in Ref.~\cite{bianchi2014complexity} lies in the choice of the remainder of the promise-defining equations, which are kept general there, while in our definition we assume $0$ in the yes-case and $2^k$ in the no-case. In our intended application of promise problems in QSQ, we want to perform deterministic measurements along a single basis. Thus, the final states in the automaton picture should be orthogonal to each other and thus lie on ``opposite sides of the Bloch sphere.'' This intuition will become clearer once we discuss the state complexity of QFAs to solve $\mathtt{DIOF^{a,k}}$.

\begin{theorem}
    An QFA can solve $\mathtt{DIOF^{a,k}}$ with $|Q|=2$ for any $a$ and $k$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Consider the QFA 
    \begin{align*}
        \mathcal{M} = ( Q &= \{ \ket{+},\ket{-} \}, \Sigma = \{\sigma_1, \dots, \sigma_H \}, \{U_{\sigma_1},\dots U_{\sigma_H}\}  \\
        Q_{yes} &= \{ \ket{+} \}, Q_{no} = \{ \ket{-}) \}, \\
        U_{\sigma_1} &= \sqrt[2^k]{Z}^{a_1}, \cdots, U_{\sigma_H} = \sqrt[2^k]{Z}^{a_H} . 
    \end{align*}
    Since the yes-instances are words solving the Diophantine equation for a total remainder of $0 \bmod{2^{k+1}}$ resulting $\forall w \in \mathtt{DIOF^{a,k}_{yes}}$ $\sqrt[2^k]{Z}^{a_1|w|_{a_1}+ \dots + a_H|w|_{a_H}} = \sqrt[2^k]{Z}^{0} = \mathbbm{1}$ and for the no-instances a remainder of $2^k \bmod{2^{k+1}}$ resulting $\sqrt[2^k]{Z}^{a_1|w|_{a_1}+ \dots + a_H|w|_{a_H}} = Z$. Hence, these words will map to orthogonal states, which we can deterministically determine with a measurement along the Pauli $X$-basis.
\end{proof}


The state complexity of a DFA solving $\mathtt{DIOF}$ can be argued from the results for $\mathtt{EVENODD}$.

\begin{theorem}
    A DFA that solves the promise problem $\mathtt{DIOF^{a,k}}$ requires at least $2^{k+1}$ states if $a_1 \nmid 2^{k+1}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof} (Adopted from \cite{bianchi2014complexity})
    We associate $\sigma_1$ with the single letter in the unary problem $\mathtt{EVENODD_k}$. Input sequences from $\mathtt{EVENODD_k}$ are clearly included in $\mathtt{DIOF^{k}}$, so the lower bound on the state complexity is already known. Left to show is that $2^{k+1}$ states are enough to accommodate non-unary input sequences. $\sigma_1$ induces a cycle with length $2^{k+1}$. The DFA solves the promise problem if the transition function of other letters is given according to the weights $a_i$, $s_n \xrightarrow{\sigma_i} s_{n+a_i}$.
\end{proof}

$\mathtt{DIOF^{a,k}}$ serves as a candidate for certification of jointly commuting gates generating finite groups, e.g., the $s$-gate and the $t$-gate (see Fig.~\ref{fig:diofst}).

   \caption{Transition graph on a Bloch-sphere for $s$ and $h$ gates on $\ket{+}$. Since $s$ implements a rotation along the Pauli $Z$-axis, it serves as a eigenoperator to $\ket{0}$ and $\ket{1}$.}
   \label{fig:blochsh}
\end{figure}

Next, we address a case of a quantum-inspired promise problem that involves ``non-commuting letters'', i.e., letters that are associated with non-commuting gates in the quantum model. This setting is sufficiently complex to necessitate the minimality toolbox we developed earlier. Let us first construct the QFA, and then translate this QFA to a DFA in the sense of Def.~\ref{def:dfafromqfa}. In the following, we will consider the QFA
\begin{align*}
    \mathcal{M}_{sh} = ( &Q = \{\ket{+},\ket{-}\}, \Sigma = \{s, h\}, \{U_{s},U_{h}\}  \\
        &Q_{yes} = \{ \ket{+} \}, Q_{no} = \{ \ket{-}) \}, \\
        U_{s} =& \begin{pmatrix}
             1&0\\
             0&i
        \end{pmatrix}, 
        U_{h} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
        \begin{pmatrix}
            1&1\\
            1&-1
        \end{pmatrix}.
\end{align*}

We study the finite Clifford group, applied to $\ket{+}$ as the initial state. We construct a DFA by associating every unique intermediate quantum state with a DFA state. As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:qfatodfa}, we reuse the Dirac notation of the quantum states of the underlying QFA as state labels of the DFA. Then, we obtain

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{A}_{sh} = (S = \{\ket{+}, \ket{-}, \ket{+_y}, \ket{-_y}, \ket{0}, \ket{1}\}, \Sigma = \{s,h\}, \delta, s_0, S_{yes} = \ket{+}, S_{no} = \ket{-}),
\end{equation} where the transition function $\delta$ can be inferred form Fig.~\ref{fig:blochsh}.
From $\mathcal{A}_{sh}$, we construct the following promise problem
\begin{theorem}
\label{def:sh}
    Consider the promise problem induced by $\mathcal{A}_{sh}$ (see. Fig.~\ref{fig:blochsh}), that is 
    \begin{align}
                \mathtt{PP}_{yes}(\mathcal{C}) &= \{w \, | \, w \in \Sigma^* \land w \ket{+} = \ket{+} \} \\
                \mathtt{PP}_{no}(\mathcal{C}) &= \{w \, | \, w \in \Sigma^* \land w \ket{+} = \ket{-} \}   
    \end{align}
    The promise problem inducing DFA is minimal in solvation mode and is thus the smallest DFA solving the promise problem.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    We use the promise-version Hopcroft's algorithm to argue the minimality of the Cayley graph in solvation mode. States $\ket{+}$ and $\ket{-}$ are final states with definite labels, and we show for all possible label assignments of the remaining states $\ket{+_y},\ket{-_y},\ket{0},\ket{1}$ that the DFA version of the Cayley graph is minimal, i.e., all states are reachable and distinguishable. Reachability is obvious; we proceed immediately to the check of distinguishability. In this case, distinguishability of all states follows because all pairs within the automaton are reachable from the distinguishable pair  $\ket{+}$, and $\ket{-}$: 
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $\ket{+}$ and $\ket{+_y}$: If $\ket{+_y} \in S_{no}$, distinguishable under the empty word; if $\ket{+_y} \in S_{yes}$, $\ket{+}\xrightarrow{s} \ket{+_y}$, and $\ket{+_y}\xrightarrow{s} \ket{-}$. \textit{Distinguishable}.
        
        \item $\ket{+}$ and $\ket{-}$: $\ket{-} \in S_{no}$, distinguishable under the empty word. \textit{Distinguishable}.
        
       \item $\ket{+}$ and $\ket{-_y}$: If $\ket{-_y} \in S_{no}$, distinguishable under the empty word; if $\ket{-_y} \in S_{yes}$, $\ket{-_y}\xrightarrow{s} \ket{+}$, and $\ket{+}\xrightarrow{s} \ket{+_y}$. If $\ket{+_y} \in S_{no}$, distinguishable, else $\ket{-_y}\xrightarrow{ss} \ket{+_y}$ and $\ket{+}\xrightarrow{ss} \ket{-}$. \textit{Distinguishable}.

        \item $\ket{+_y}$ and $\ket{-_y}$:  $\ket{+_y}\xrightarrow{s} \ket{-}$, and $\ket{-_y}\xrightarrow{s} \ket{+}$. \textit{Distinguishable}.

        \item $\ket{-}$ and $\ket{+_y}$ or $\ket{-_y}$: analogous to $\ket{+}$ and $\ket{+_y}$ or $\ket{-_y}$. \textit{Distinguishable}.

        \item $\ket{0}$ and $\ket{+}, \ket{+_y}, \ket{-_y}, \ket{-}$: applying $s^*$ on $\ket{0}$ leaves this state invariant. We can always distinguish these states using an appropriate unary $s$ word. \textit{Distinguishable}.

        \item $\ket{1}$ and $\ket{+}, \ket{+_y}, \ket{-_y}, \ket{-}$: analogous to the case above. \textit{Distinguishable}.

        \item $\ket{0}$ and $\ket{1}$: $\ket{0}\xrightarrow{h} \ket{+}$, and $\ket{1}\xrightarrow{h} \ket{-}$. \textit{Distinguishable}.
        
        \end{itemize}
        
        All states are distinguishable in solvation mode, and thus, the solution to the induced promise problem is given by the automaton itself.
\end{proof}

Since the QFA clearly solves the same promise problem with only two state dimensions, we constructed a promise problem with state advantage for the quantum system from non-commuting gates. Ref.~\cite{noller2024classical} demonstrated a QSQ for Clifford group generators.
We also note that DFA-induced promise problems can be formulated equivalently but differently using regular expressions, potentially offering greater insight depending on the background of the reader. Just as every regular language corresponds to a DFA and vice versa, both the yes- and no-languages can likewise be expressed in regular expression format. For the ${s,h}$ case, this becomes, for example,

\begin{align}\label{eq:regex}
    \mathtt{PP_{yes}} &= \{(hs^*h|s((h|s(hs^*h)^*s)h)^*(h|s(hs^*h)^*s)s)^*\} \\
    \mathtt{PP_{no}} &= \{(hs^*h|s((h|s(hs^*h)^*s)h)^*(h|s(hs^*h)^*s)s)^*s((h|s(hs^*h)^*s)h)^*s(hs^*h)^*\}.
\end{align}   

The DFA to regular expression transformation was performed using the web tool~\cite{ivan2024fsm2regex}. Here, the Kleene star $^*$ indicates repetition of the element, or the entire expression when applied outside a parenthesis, any number of times, and $|$ a OR, i.e., $(a|b)$ indicates $a$ or $b$ either the expression left or right from $|$. Since DFAs as they are commonly defined in computer science do not have $\mathtt{indefinite}$ states, we generate the Expressions~\ref{eq:regex} in two steps, once we treat the both, the $\mathtt{reject}$  and $\mathtt{indefinite}$ states of $\mathcal{A}_{sh}$ as $\mathtt{reject}$  to obtain $\mathtt{PP_{yes}}$ and as the second step we treat the $\mathtt{accept}$ and $\mathtt{indefinite}$ state as $\mathtt{reject}$  states and the $\mathtt{reject}$  state as $\mathtt{accept}$ to generate $\mathtt{PP_{no}}$.


\section{Generalized Promise Problems}

Up to now, we have examined promise problems, which are, by definition, dichotomic. We have a dichotomous scenario in a qubit system with a single measurement setting. However, for multi-qubit or qudit systems, this is not the case canonically. Thus, we adjust the promise problem to accommodate a multichotomous setting.

\begin{definition} (Multichotomous promise problems)

A multichotomous promise problem is given by sets of words $G = (G_0, \dots, G_n)$ with pairwise disjoint $G_0 \cap \dots \cap G_n = \emptyset$ and $G_0 \cup \dots \cup G_{n-1} \subseteq \Sigma^* $. 
It is solved by a mulichotomous promise version DFA $\mathcal{A} = (S,\Sigma,\delta,s_0,S_0, \dots, S_{n-1})$ if for every $w \in G_0 \cup \dots \cup G_{n-1} $ it holds
    \begin{align*}
        w \in G_0 &\Rightarrow \delta(s_0, w) \in S_0 \\
        &\vdotswithin{=} \notag \\
        w \in G_{n-1} &\Rightarrow \delta(s_0, w) \in S_{n-1}. \\
    \end{align*}
We say a DFA recognizes the promise problem if the input space covers the entire set of all words and the DFA fulfills a biconditional relation, i.e., $w \in G_i \iff \delta(s_0, w) \in S_i$, and say a DFA solves the promise problem if the input space is restricted to the promise languages fulfilling the relation $w \in G_i \implies \delta(s_0, w) \in S_i$. We call $G$ an $n$-chotomous promise problem.
\end{definition}

In the following, we propose multichotomous promise problems with a dimensional quantum advantage. We define such promise problems by expanding the previous $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$ -- first, we examine $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$ on one qudit, $\mathtt{GEVENODD^r_q}$, and then $\mathtt{EVENODD_k}$ on multiple qubits, $\operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD_k}}$. We will also discuss the minimality of DFAs for multichotomous promise problems and use these arguments to devise non-unary promise problems of systems beyond qubits.

\subsection{Generalizations of $\mathtt{EVENODD}$}

\begin{definition}\label{def:geven}
    The generalized promise problem $\mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_q = \\(\mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_0,\mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_1, \dots, \mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_{r-1})$ with $q$ prime comprises the following unary input strings
    \begin{align}
       \mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_0 &= \{ \sigma^{i\cdot r} | i \equiv 0\ (\bmod q)\} \\
       \mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_1 &= \{ \sigma^{i\cdot r} | i \equiv 1\ (\bmod q)\} \\
       &\vdotswithin{=} \notag \\
       \mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_{q-1} &= \{ \sigma^{i\cdot r} | i \equiv q-1\ (\bmod q )\}.
    \end{align}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}
    The generalized promise problem $\mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_q$ can be solved with a $(4+q)$-tupled DFA $\mathcal{A} = (S,\Sigma,\delta,s_0,S_0,\dots,S_{q-1})$ with at least
    \begin{equation}
        |S| = q^{m_q + 1},
    \end{equation}
    where $m_q$ the multiplicity of the prime factor $q$ in $r = q^{m_q}\cdot r'$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Analogous to the proof of Theorem \ref{theorem:originalpp}. If $r$ and $q$ are co-prime, a $\bmod \,q $ operation on the input sequence length is enough to differentiate $\mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_0, \dots,\mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_{q-1}$. This is no longer the case if $q$ occurs in the prime factors of $r$. Express $r = r'\cdot q^{m_q}$, we are looking for $l$ such that $\{ (n\cdot q + 0)\cdot r'\cdot q^{m_q} \bmod{l}\}\cap\{ (n\cdot q + 1)\cdot r'\cdot q^{m_q} \bmod{l}\}\cap\{ (n\cdot q + 2)\cdot r'\cdot q^{m_q} \bmod{l}\} \cap \dots \cap \{ (n\cdot q + q -1)\cdot r'\cdot q^{m_q}\bmod{l} \} = \emptyset$ pairwise disjoint for all $n\in \mathbb{N}$. 
    
    Pairwise disjoint sets are given by $ l = q^{m_q + 1}$: 
    \begin{align*}
        &\{ (n q + 0)r' q^{m_q} \bmod q^{m_q+1} \equiv 0 \}\cap
        \{(nq + 1)r' q^{m_q} \bmod q^{m_q + 1} \equiv m \}\cap \\  
        &\{(n q + 2) r' q^{m_q} \bmod{q^{m_q + 1}} \equiv 2m \} 
        \cap\dots \cap \{(n q + q-1) r' q^{m_q} \bmod{q^{m_q + 1}} \equiv (q-1) m\}
    \end{align*}
    Left to show is that $0 \not\equiv m \not\equiv \dots \not\equiv (q-1)\cdot m$. Note that $ a \equiv b \bmod{ c }\iff k\cdot a \equiv k\cdot b \bmod{ k}\cdot c$. Hence, we can reduce $m \equiv r'\cdot q^{m_q} (\bmod{ q^{m_q+1}})$ to $m' \equiv r' (\bmod {q})$. For multiples of $r'$, it holds
    \begin{align*}
        u \cdot r' \equiv v \cdot r'  \Rightarrow (u - v)\cdot r' \equiv 0 ,
    \end{align*}
    and $r'$ and $q$ co-prime by definition. For $u,v < q$, the only solution is $u =w$. Which shows that $\bmod{ q^{m_q + 1}}$ solves the promise problem. Any smaller choice of the modulus $l$ causes a contradiction because $l\cdot r \equiv 0$ but $\sigma^{l\cdot r} \not\in \mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_0$ unless $q | l$, which would, however, map all sequences to $0$.
    
\end{proof}


\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[scale=0.6]{images/automata/trit.pdf}
   \caption{$\mathtt{GEVENODD}^rq$ for $r=3$ and $q=3$. The initial state is the $+1$ eigenstate of $X_q$ (Eq.\ref{eq:genX}), the transitions are rotations with the depicted order, and measurements are in the $X_q$-basis. The promise problem is formulated such that we obtain deterministic outcomes since test sequences map to eigenstates of $X_q$. Compared to the dichotomic $\mathtt{EVENODD_k}$, we have three kinds of output labels (the double circle corresponding to $\ket{X_0}$, the dark-filled circle to $\ket{X_1}$, and the stripe-filled circle to $\ket{X_2}$), not counting the $\mathtt{indefinite}$ category.}
   \label{fig:geven3}
\end{figure}

\begin{theorem}
    The promise problem $\mathtt{GEVENODD}^r_q$ can be solved by a $(4+q)$-tupled QFA $\mathcal{M} = (Q, \Sigma, \{U_\sigma \,|\, \sigma \in \Sigma \}, \ket{0}, Q_{0}, \dots, Q_{q-1}) $ with $|Q|=q$. 
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
    Consider the following QFA
        \begin{align}
        \mathcal{M} = ( Q &= \{\ket{X_0},\dots,\ket{X_{q-1}} \},\, \Sigma = \{\sigma\}, \{U_\sigma\},\, \ket{X_0},\, \\ Q_{0} &= \{\ket{X_0}\},\dots, Q_{q-1} = \{\ket{X_{q-1}}\} ) ,
    \end{align}
    where $\ket{X_0},\dots,\ket{X_{q-1}}$ are eigenstates of the $q$-dimensional $X$- or shift operator \cite{pudda2024generalised}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:genX}
        X_q = 
        \begin{pmatrix}
            0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 1 \\
            1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\
            0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\
            \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\
            0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 & 0 \\
        \end{pmatrix}.
    \end{equation}  
    We choose $U_\sigma := \sqrt[r]{Z_q}$, where
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:genZ}
        Z_q = 
        \begin{pmatrix}
            1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\
            0 & w & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\
            0 & 0 & w^2 & \cdots & 0 \\
            \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
            0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & w^{q-1}
        \end{pmatrix},
    \end{equation}
    where $w = e^{i \frac{2\pi}{q}}$, the unit root.
    Then, $\ket{X_0} \xrightarrow{\sqrt[r]{Z_q}^r} \ket{X_1} \xrightarrow{\sqrt[r]{Z_q}^r} \cdots \xrightarrow{\sqrt[r]{Z_q}^r} \ket{X_{q-1}}$ resulting in a QFA uniquely mapping input sequences to corresponding output states.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Multi-qubit case of $\mathtt{EVENODD}$}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[scale=0.75]{images/automata/neven.pdf}
   \caption{$\operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD_k}}$ for $k=1$ and $\mathtt{N} = 2$ (or $s$-gates on two qubits). The initial state is $\ket{++}$, which we associate with count $\mathtt{00}$ and the double circle. $\ket{-+}$ is the filled circle, $\ket{+-}$ is the dashed circle and $\ket{--}$ is the filled and dashed circle. The counts reflect the $s$ counts to reach the respective states modulo 4. The final states of the DFA are exactly these marked states, which are the eigenstates of $X\otimes X$.}
   \label{fig:neven3}
\end{figure}

\begin{definition}\label{def:neven}
    The ``multi-qubit'' version of $\mathtt{EVENODD_k}$ is called $\operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD_k}}= \linebreak (\operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD^k_{b_0\dotsb_{N-1}}}},b_i\in\{0,1\})$ and is given by following sets of sequences
    \begin{align}
        \operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD^k_{00\dots0}}} &= \{ |\sigma_0| \equiv 0 \land  |\sigma_1| \equiv 0, \dots |\sigma_{N-1}| \equiv 0 \} \\
        \operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD^k_{00\dots1}}} &= \{ |\sigma_0| \equiv 0 \land  |\sigma_1| \equiv 0, \dots |\sigma_{N-1}| \equiv 2^k \} \\
        &\vdots\\
        \operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD^k_{11\dots1}}} &= \{ |\sigma_0| \equiv 2^k \land  |\sigma_1| \equiv 2^k, \dots |\sigma_{N-1}| \equiv 2^k \} ,
    \end{align}
    where the alphabet is given by $\Sigma = \{\sigma_0, \cdots, \sigma_{N-1} \}$ and congruence with modulo $2^{k+1}$.
\end{definition}

We start with the quantum solution to this promise problem to provide intuition.

\begin{theorem}
    The promise problem $\operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD_k}}$ can be solved with a $N$-qubit QFA $\mathcal{M} = (Q, \Sigma, \{U_{\sigma_i|\sigma_i \in \Sigma}\}, \ket{0}, Q_{00\dots0},Q_{00\dots1},\dots,Q_{11\dots1})$ with $|Q| = 2^N$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
    Consider the QFA where classifying states $Q_{b_0\dots b_{N-1}}$ are given by the $N-$qubit Pauli $X$ basis states
    $Q_{00\dots0} = \ket{++\cdots+}, Q_{00\dots1} = \ket{++\cdots-},\dots,Q_{11\dots1} = \ket{--\cdots-}$.
    If we choose $U_{\sigma_i} = \sqrt[2^k]{Z_i}$ we obtain the desired behavior.  
\end{proof}

To accomplish the same task with a classical DFA, we also require a multiplicative scaling compared to the ``single-qubit  " $\mathtt{EVENODD_k}$ dimension requirement.

\begin{theorem}
    A DFA that solves the promise problem $\operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD_k}}$ requires at least $2^{N(k+1)}$ states.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    First, we devise a DFA with $2^{N(k+1)}$ that solves the promise problem. We notice that $\operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD_k}}$ can be solved by counting the number of each letter $\sigma_i$ in modulo $2^{k+1}$. The modulo counting is implemented by a cyclic transition of length $2^{k+1}$. Then, we can label each state with the current gate counts. To represent all possible gate counts of $N$ different gates in $\operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD_k}}$, we can use $N$ base $2^{k+1}$ numbers. If we include all possible $2^{N(k+1)}$ count instances, we can faithfully process all possible input sequences, even beyond the promised inputs.
    Next, we prove there is no smaller DFA that solves the promise problem. If $|S|<2^{N(k+1)}$, there is at least one state $s$ that has at least two inequivalent count labels, $l_1,l_2$. Let's call the input sequence leading to $s$ with count $l_1$ and $l_2$, $p_1$ and $p_2$, respectively. We call the inverse of an input sequences $p \notin \operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD^k_{00\dots0}}}$ to be sequences such that  $p^{-1}\cdot p \in  \operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD^k_{00\dots0}}}$. Then, $p_1 \cdot p_2^{-1}$ is an input that reaches a state that is from the category $S_{00\dots0}$. However, generally $p_1 \cdot p_2^{-1} \notin \operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD^k_{00\dots0}}}$ causing a contradiction. The only case where this would be true is if $p_1 = p_2$, which we have excluded. The last step is to repeat the sequence $p_1 \cdot p_2^{-1}$ such that this generic sequence becomes compatible with the promised inputs. This can be achieved in the worst case with $2^k$ repetitions of $p_1 \cdot p_2^{-1}$. Note that it may happen that $2^k\cdot(p_1 \cdot p_2^{-1}) \in \operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD^k_{00\dots0}}}$. Then, repeat only $2^{k-1}$ times.
\end{proof}


\subsection{Non-unary multichotomous promise problems}

Technically, $\operatorname{\mathtt{N-EVENODD^k}}$ is an instance of a non-unary multichotomous promise problem, but in this section, we want to discuss generalizations of the quantum-inspired promise problems defined from known QFAs from finite group generators. 
We start by expanding the notion of minimality to multichotomous promise version DFAs. For this, we only need to modify the definition of state equivalence.

\begin{definition} (Equivalence of two states in a $n$-chotomous DFA) Two states of a given $n$-chotomous DFA $\mathcal{A}$, $s_i, s_j \in S$, $i\neq j$ are equivalent or indistinguishable if $\forall w\in \Sigma^*$, $\delta(s_i,w)$ and $\delta(s_j,w)$ belong to the same category, i.e. $S_0, \dots, S_{n-1}$.
\end{definition}

Applying this equivalence relation to the notion and methods for minimization (Alg.~\ref{alg:dfamin}-\ref{alg:mindfasolv}), these concepts become valid for the multichotomous case. Practically, the change we see in the promise-version Hopcroft algorithm for minimization in solvation mode is that the SAT problem on binary variables becomes a SAT problem with $n$-valued variables.

\subsubsection{The promise problem $\mathtt{MUB}^q$}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics{images/automata/mub3.pdf}
   \caption{$\mathcal{A}^{MUB}_{q}$ for $q=3$. We have $q^2$ states and $q+1$ letters/gates. We have $q$ $q$-cycles, where each cycle is indicated by the letter that acts as an eigenoperator. Other letters besides $H$ span on these $q$ states $q$-cycles with varying step sizes; for $q=3$, we have either step size $1$ or $2$. $H$ maps the eigenstates of each $q$-cycle to another cycle, thus creating an additional $q$ $q$-cycles.}
   \label{fig:mub3}
\end{figure}

We consider the QFA generated by the gates $\{H_q,X_qZ_q^i\}_{i=0}^{q-1}$, applied to the $+1$ eigenstate of the generalized $X_q$, $\ket{X_q}$. Explicit forms of $X_q$ and $Z_q$ are given in Eq.~\ref{eq:genX} and Eq.~\ref{eq:genZ}, respectively. $H_q$ is the generalized Hadamard gate fulfilling the relation $H_q X_q H_q^\dagger = Z_q$. The explicit matrix reads \cite{pudda2024generalised}
\begin{equation}
    H_q = \frac{1}{\sqrt{q}}
    \begin{pmatrix}
            1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\
            1 & w^{q-1} & w^{2(q-1)} & \cdots & w^{(q-1)^2} \\
            1 & w^{q-2} & w^{2(q-2)} & \cdots & w^{(q-1)(q-2)} \\
            \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
            1 & w & w^{2} & \cdots & w^{q-1},
    \end{pmatrix}    
\end{equation}
    where $w = e^{i \frac{2\pi}{q}}$, the unit root. Then, these gates generate a finite group, and applied to the initial state $\ket{0_q}$, we can obtain a DFA $\mathcal{A}^{MUB}_{q}$ from the $q^2$ unique intermediate states (cf. Fig.~\ref{fig:mub3}). The initial state is stabilized by $X_q$, which is indicated via a self-loop for that gate. Gates $\{X_qZ_q^i\}_{i=1}^{q-1}$ each induce a $q$-cycle mapping between all $q$ eigenstates of $X_q$. Each of them has order $q$ since $q$ is prime, but different ``step sizes''. $H_q$ maps the $X_q$ eigenstates to $X_q Z_q$ eigenstates (up to a global phase) that span again a $q$-cycle with self-loops. In summary, we have $q$ $q$-cycles, where each cycle consists of $q$-cycles from $q$ gates, and self-loops on the $q$ states by one gate, and $H_q$ generates $q$ $q$-cycles, mapping between the stabilized cycles. We define the measurement basis along $X_q$. Let us define a promise problem from this structure:

    
    \begin{definition} \label{def:mub} ($\mathtt{MUB^q}$)
        Given the \ac{DFA}  $\mathcal{A}^{MUB}_{q}$ defined from a \ac{QFA} with acting gates $\{H_q,X_qZ_q^i\}_{i=0}^{q-1}$ on the initial state $\ket{0}$, the $+1$ eigenstate of $X_q$, paired with a final measurement along $X_q$, i.e. projection operation on states $\{\ket{0},\ket{1},\dots,\ket{q-1}\}$ we can define the $q$-chotomous promise problem as
        \begin{align}
            \mathtt{MUB^q}_0  &= \{ w \in \Sigma^* | \delta(\ket{0},w) = w \} \\
            \mathtt{MUB^q}_1 &= \{ w \in \Sigma^* | \delta(\ket{0},w) = \ket{1} \} \\
            &\vdots \\
            \mathtt{MUB^q}_{q-1} &= \{ w \in \Sigma^* | \delta(\ket{0},w) = \ket{q-1} \}
        \end{align}
    \end{definition}

To infer the smallest \ac{DFA} solution to $\mathtt{MUB^q}$, let us study whether $\mathcal{A}^{MUB}_{q}$ is minimal.

\begin{theorem}
    $\mathcal{A}^{MUB}_{q}$ is minimal in solvation mode.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    We run the promise-version Hopcroft method to infer the minimality of $\mathcal{A}^{MUB}_{q}$. We check mutual distinguishability for each unique pair. The labeled states in $\mathcal{A}^{MUB}_{q}$ are the eigenstates of $X_q$, each with a distinct label; therefore, all pairs within this $q$-cycle are distinguishable. Starting from the pair $(\ket{0},\ket{1})$ and applying consecutive $H_q$ transitions, we infer that states sharing the same eigenspace are distinguishable. This accounts for $q^2$ pairs among $\binom{q^2}{2}$ total pairs.
    We observe that all other pairs cannot be definitively assigned to a label but belong to the same cluster. Choose the seed pair $(\ket{0}, \delta(\ket{0},H_q))$, where $\ket{0}$ is an eigenstate of $X_q$ and $\delta(\ket{0},H_q)$ is an eigenstate of $X_q Z_q$. By applying words consisting of gates $X_q$ and $X_q Z_q$, we learn that all pairs within $X_q$ eigenstates and $X_q Z_q$ eigenstates are within the same cluster, and likewise all states of any two $q$-cycles with one $H_q$ distance. 
    
    This cluster cannot be made compatible with the indistinguishable cluster, since the clusters are only mergeable if $l_{\ket{0}}= l_{\delta(\ket{0},H_q)}$ and $l_{\ket{1}}= l_{\delta(\ket{1},H_q)}$, but we know $l_{\ket{0}} \neq l_{\ket{1}}$, so this cluster is not mergeable. We have $\frac{q-1}{2}$ such clusters, and these conflicting equations can be constructed analogously. Thus, $\mathcal{A}^{MUB}_{q}$ is minimal in solvation mode.
\end{proof}
$\mathtt{MUB^q}$ is by definition solved by a \ac{QFA} with $|Q| = q$, thus we have a quadratic dimension advantage in the quantum case. We conjecture that such protocols can be used to certify $q$-dimensional quantum models.

\section{Finite promise problems}

So far, we have derived promise problems that contain, in principle, infinitely long words from certain quantum models. This is very inconvenient if we want to invert the picture and test these input sequences to certify the occurrence of a certain quantum model. However, we know that the investigated quantum model is defined on a finite group -- this means the underlying structure is finite even though individual words can be arbitrarily long. Thus, in principle, we only need to include enough test sequences until the periodic structure of the finite group becomes apparent. Conversely, if we do not include enough test sequences from the infinite set, there may exist DFA solutions to the finite promise problem requiring fewer state dimensions.
We investigate the relation between required dimensions to solve the promise problem and the choice of subset input sequences from the infinite set in the case of $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$ in the following.

\subsection{Finite $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$}

\begin{definition}\label{def:finiteevenodd}
    The finite-length version of the promise problem is given as $\mathtt{EVENODD_k^{i_{max}}} = (\mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,yes}}, \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,no}})$ 
    \begin{align}
        \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,yes}} &= \{ \sigma^{i2^k} | i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{even}} \land i \leq i_{max}\},
        \\
        \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,no}} &= \{ \sigma^{i2^k} | i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{odd} } \land i \leq i_{max} \}.
    \end{align}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}\label{theorem:finitepp}
    A DFA solving $\mathtt{EVENODD_k^{i_{max}}}$ requires the state dimension 
    \begin{equation}
        |S| = 
        \begin{cases}
            2^{k+1},\, \text{if} \enspace i_{max} \geq 2^{k+1} - 1 \\
            i_{max},\, \text{else}. 
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Since $\mathtt{EVENODD_k^{i_{max}}} \subset \mathtt{EVENODD_k}$, a DFA with $|S| = 2^{k + 1}$ solves the promise problem as per Theorem~\ref{theorem:originalpp}. Therefore, consider the case $i_{max} < 2^{k+1} - 1$. As before, examine the two input sets regarding their length per input sequence in ascending order
    \begin{align}
    \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,yes}} &= \{0, 2\cdot2^k, \dots \}, \\
        \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,no}} &= \{1\cdot2^k, 3\cdot2^k, \dots \}.
    \end{align}
    The promise problem is solved if the DFA $\mathcal{A}$ maps the two distinct sets into distinct sets $\mathcal{A}(\mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,yes}}) \cap \mathcal{A}(\mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,no} }) = \emptyset $.
    Let's assume first that $\mathcal{A}$ is a tail-free DFA. Then,
    \begin{align}
        \mathcal{A}(\mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,yes}}) &= \{0, 2\cdot m, \dots \}, \\
        \mathcal{A}(\mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,no}}) &= \{1\cdot2^k \bmod l  =: m, 3\cdot m, \dots \}.
    \end{align} 

    These outputs induce another cycle on the DFA states $0 \rightarrow 1\cdot m \rightarrow 2\cdot m \rightarrow 3 \cdot m \rightarrow \dots$, where $\mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,yes}}$-states and $\mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,no}}$-states are visited alternately. If $l = 2\cdot 2^k$, the output cycle has length $l_o = 2$, consisting of states $0$ and $2^k$ solving the infinite promise problem. If $l<2\cdot 2^k$ and, for simplicity, $l \nmid 2^k$, we know that the first $l_o$ sequences can be correctly represented. Odd $l_o$ would cause a conflict at the $l_o+1$-th sequence.  Since this output cycle is defined on $l$ states with step size $m$, the cycle length is the smallest combination of $p,l_o$ such that $p\cdot l = l_o\cdot m$. This means for $\gcd(l,m)=g$ we have $p \cdot g \cdot l' = l_o \cdot g \cdot m'$, $l_o = l'$. Note that $l_o$ is always odd -- the only scenario of $l_o$ even would occur if $l$ is some even number $l = 2^i\cdot l'$ and $\gcd(l,m)$ odd or of lower multiplicity than $i$ of the prime factor $2$. However since $2^k = n \cdot l + m \Rightarrow 2^i(2^{k-i} + l') = m$ which means $2^i$ is a common divisor of both $m$ and $l$ and thus $l_o$ odd. Hence, such a DFA can only accommodate a promise problem with up to $l_o$ sequences. 
    Next, we will see that in case $l_o \neq l$, we can increase the maximum sequence number to $|S|$ by adding a tail of length $t = 1$, such as $l = |S| - 1 $. From $2^k = n \cdot g \cdot l' + g \cdot m' = n\cdot g (l' + m')$ we see that $\gcd(l,m)$ can only be power of $2$. Hence, if  $\gcd(l,m) \neq 1 \Rightarrow \gcd(l-1, \tilde{m}) = 1$. Since single tail state can correctly recognize the empty string, such a DFA can account for $|S|$ sequences in total. Thus, a promise problem with $i_{max} < 2^{k+1} - 1$ can be solved with a DFA with $|S| = i_{max}$ states.
    \end{proof}



\begin{corollary}
If $i_{max} \leq 2^{k+1}-1$, it is enough to consider sequences for $i_{max}$ even
\begin{align}
          \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,yes}} &= \{ \sigma^{i2^k} | i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{even}} \land i \leq i_{max}\}, \\
           \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,no}} &= \{ \sigma^{1 \cdot 2^k} \},
\end{align}

and for  $i_{max}$ odd
\begin{align}
          \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,yes}} &= \{ \sigma^{0}, \sigma^{2 \cdot 2^k}\}, \\
         \mathtt{EVENODD^{i_{max}}_{k,no}} &= \{ \sigma^{i2^k} | i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0,\,\mathrm{odd} } \land i \leq i_{max} \}.
\end{align}
\end{corollary}

We presented several promise problems that demonstrate succinctness for quantum finite automata, as summarized in Table \ref{tab:PP-overview}. Our exploration of quantum finite state automata was motivated by the serendipitous discovery that the independently developed QSQ protocol -- which turns out to be an instance of the "word discrimination problem" using quantum models with dimensional advantage -- had been examined in the late 1990s and early 2000s in the context of promise problem versions of language recognition.

Initially, we lacked tools to prove the classical dimension of these computational tasks. For sufficiently small systems, dimensional separation has been shown by optimizing over all two-dimensional Markov chains to demonstrate the non-existence of a suitable classical solution. Only with the notion of quantum finite-state automata could we provide a clearer insight into the source of the quantum memory advantage. 

Notably, our findings reveal that advantages exist when the promise problem underlies a finite group structure with more elements than the quantum model dimension, and the promise problem is chosen such that the output statistic cannot be obtained via its subgroup. The product of this automaton learning task is effectively given as the Cayley graph of the underlying finite group. In the context of QSQ, however, the aim is to certify in a semi-device-independent manner the gates, initial state, and measurement, where a unitary gauge is unavoidable due to its invariance in the measurement statistics. Since the gate can only be inferred from the learned Cayley graph, all that can be certified is that the implemented gates are generators of the learned group, leaving the specific instance of the generator unidentified. For example, in the main text we mention $T$-gate certification with $\mathtt{EVENODD}_2$: we infer from the minimal solution to the promise problem that the underlying group is equivalent to  $\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z}_8$, yet we cannot pinpoint which "step-size" the gate has; i.e., it could be any number smaller than 8 that does not divide 8, all possible generators of $\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z}_8$. This outcome introduces a freedom that exceeds that of a unitary gauge. Consequently, one may either adopt more general gauges or focus on cases where the group generators are almost unique, such as those for the $s$-gate or the combination of $s$ and $h$.

Beyond this discussion, many open questions remain in establishing equivalence between QSQ and promise problems with dimensional advantages, which we summarize below.

\begin{itemize}
    \item We studied the dimension requirements for finite instances of $\mathtt{EVENODD}_k$. What is the effect of finiteness in other promise problems, particularly for non-commutative gates? How many sequences are sufficient to characterize non-commutative behavior?
    \item Another point regarding finiteness: we know from Refs.~\cite {noller2024classical,noller2024self} that we do not require as many test sequences in the finite promise problems such that the solving DFA becomes equivalent to the full QFA. It seems that it suffices to include enough solutions until the two-dimensional DFA is excluded. 
    We conjecture that in QSQ, "valid solutions" to the promise problems reduce to graphs with single $\mathtt{accept}$ and $\mathtt{reject}$  states, so we can only confuse the solution with its subgroup. Other-dimensional solutions occurring from the finite choice of test sequences are implicitly ruled out.
    \item We already know that QSQ is possible for a universal gate set \cite{noller2024classical}. Although we worked only with finite group languages here, finiteness is apparently not a necessary requirement. We conjecture that for certain finitely generated infinite graphs, a partial graph suffices to characterize the underlying group. Devising such minimal partial graphs would lead to new instances of quantum-inspired promise problem.
    \item A more practical open task involves defining minimal sets for QSQ: What are the statistical and robustness implications of test sequence selection? Can we obtain better and tighter fidelity bounds by measuring more sequences? What are the effects of finite statistics, and are there dependencies on the type of hardware noise?
\end{itemize}

What the study of promise problems demonstrates is that quantum advantage in this setting stems from the qubit's ability to encode intermediate states within the superposition of a single qubit, while a memory-restricted classical computer must resolve them explicitly. Quantum advantages in memory-restricted tasks have been studied in Ref.~\cite{maslov2020quantum} for symmetric Boolean functions. The problem we have been solving can be reformulated as a learning problem of a Boolean function from given input-output patterns.

It would also be interesting to connect this problem to hidden subgroup problems. The hidden subgroup problem is stated as follows \cite{lomont2004the}: Given a group $G$, a subgroup $H \leq G$, a set $X$, and a function $f: G \to X$ that \textit{hides} the subgroup $H$ if $\forall g_1, g_2 \in G$, $f(g_1) = f(g_2)$ if and only if $g_1H = g_2H$, the task is to determine the generating set for $H$ using an oracle implementing $f$.
Our setting is certainly not a hidden subgroup problem, but borrowing the same language, we want to determine the generating set of a group given an oracle implementation of $f$. We require that this function be chosen such that it strictly does not contain a hidden subgroup, since that would lead to more than one compatible group solving our learning task, which would not serve as a certificate for certain quantum gates. Recently, it has been shown that verifying the order of a finite group given as a black box is in the complexity class QCMA \cite{gall2025group}. We believe that establishing connections to these fields will surely be enlightening.

Fortunately or unfortunately, the author's time as a PhD student is over, but we put down the pen full in the hope that these notes might spark new curiosity in some reader.
